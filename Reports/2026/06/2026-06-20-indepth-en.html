Executive Summary

On November 27, 2025 we published our 2026 Coffee Market Annual Outlook. Seven months on, the first half of this report grades it honestly. In short: we got the direction right (the shift to a bearish, oversupplied regime) but the magnitude and path (price level and climate assumption) badly wrong.

We made a record Brazil 2026/27 crop and the first surplus after four deficits our core case — and it materialized (USDA 71.9M; StoneX global 182.5M, surplus >10M). The 0% US tariff hold, limited 2026 EUDR impact, and a structurally higher floor ("no return below 200¢") all hit. But two big calls missed. First, we set the price level too high — versus a base of 320–360¢, arabica fell to a 19-month low of 243.90¢ in June, undershooting even the Bear scenario (250–300¢) we assigned just 30% probability. Second, our climate assumption flipped — we bet on "La Niña 61%," but an El Niño formed and was confirmed (JMA, NOAA, Australia's BOM).

In one line: we wrote "the descent is rough," but the actual descent was steeper and took the opposite ridge (El Niño). The second half rebuilds the road from that lesson, around four H2 drivers — surplus realization, the low-stock shield, the Sept–Oct flowering, and the EUDR D-Day.

What Went Wrong — Three Lessons

1. The climate base-rate trap — overfitting a "probability"

The most painful miss was climate. In November, most models pointed to La Niña and we assigned 61%. Instead the equatorial Pacific flipped to El Niño, confirmed in June by the JMA, NOAA (67% super-El Niño) and Australia's BOM. Lesson: seasonal climate forecasts have wide confidence bands at a six-month horizon. Rather than concentrating scenario weight on a single regime, we should have held symmetric optionality across both regimes.

2. Overtrusting the inflation premium — surplus crushed the cushion

We anchored a price floor near 320¢ on rising production costs and a risk premium. The floor (no break below 200¢) held, but the 100¢+ cushion above it evaporated in front of the regime shift to surplus. Once the record crop was confirmed, speculators flipped net short and price ran straight to the Bear scenario. Lesson: when the balance shifts from deficit to surplus, cost/premium logic supports the "floor" but not the "level."

3. Underweighting the bear case — 30% became reality

We put 30% on the Bear (250–300¢) and 50% on the base (320–360¢). H1 went through the Bear and below. Lesson: the first surplus after four straight deficits is a regime change, not mean reversion. In regime-change phases, tail-scenario probabilities should be structurally raised — markets find the new equilibrium fast, and often overshoot.

Lessons combined: our frame ("the descent is rough") was right. What we got wrong was the speed of the descent and the starting altitude. A correct direction call can still flip P&L if level, path and climate assumptions miss. The H2 outlook reflects this by offering driver-led paths rather than a fixed price grid.

The core tension is "future surplus" vs "present low stocks." Brazil's record crop (USDA 71.9M, Marex 75.9M) flows at the July–September peak, turning the global balance to surplus after four deficits. Yet ICE certified stocks sit at a 2.25-year low and spot stays tight on producer withholding. H2 price is decided on the tug-of-war between these forces — and which way the four drivers below tip it.

① Surplus realization — H2's gravity

The baseline gravity points down. StoneX forecasts 2026/27 global output at a record 182.5M bags (from 170.1M), the first surplus (>10M) after four straight deficits. Rabobank, citing an 8.64M surplus, sees arabica falling roughly a further third by Q4. As Brazil's new crop peaks in July–September and withholding starts to ease, this surplus narrative gets priced into spot.

② The low-stock shield — what holds the floor

But a shield resists the gravity. ICE certified stocks sit at a 2.25-year low (394,267 bags), and consumer/origin stocks are historically thin. Falling stocks despite a bumper crop sustains spot tightness and backwardation, letting the surplus narrative pull price down only in measured steps. The thicker the low-stock shield, the more every supply-disruption signal is amplified — it supports the floor while raising volatility.

③ The Sept–Oct flowering — H2's biggest wildcard

Paradoxically, El Niño lowers June–July frost risk (a warming bias) — weakening one traditional H2 upside catalyst. The real wildcard is the Sept–Oct flowering. If El Niño-driven heat and erratic rain disrupt flowering and fruit set, the 2027/28 crop takes the hit (per USDA/StoneX warnings). The 2026/27 record is already priced, so H2 upside optionality rests almost entirely on the "flowering weather → 27/28 fears" channel. Minas Gerais Sept–Oct rainfall and temperatures are the season's pivot.

④ EUDR D-Day — the December 30 cliff

As our November piece projected, the EUDR large-operator start date held at December 30, 2026 (SMEs 27.6.30). So H2 is when the regulation shifts from "delayed" to "imminent." As year-end nears, expect ① demand skew toward traceable lots and a modest premium, and ② a bifurcation between EUDR-compliant and non-compliant supply. At the same time, further-delay/simplification risk is live — the Commission filed a simplification review by April 30, 2026, and parts of the trade flag another possible delay. Enforcement implies a premium (up); another delay means supply stability (down).

⑤ Macro/geopolitics — two-way winds

Late in H1 the dollar hit a 13-month high and the real weakened back to 5.18. A weaker real incentivizes producer selling (down) — yet price still bounced, showing supply/geopolitical upside outweighed it. US tariffs are ~0% but a Section 122 surcharge is in legal limbo on appeal (fluid), and the Strait of Hormuz closure feeds cost-side upside via freight, insurance and fuel. The 5.10–5.30 FX band and geopolitical headlines are the main sources of near-term H2 volatility.

⑥ Demand — the quiet downside risk

Overshadowed by supply, two-plus years of high prices are eroding consumption. Elevated retail prices, roaster robusta substitution (downgrading), and macro softness could deepen the surplus and accelerate the downside. With Vietnam 25/26 exports +7.9% and output +6%, robusta supply is ample — leaving room for substitution.

The H2 Price Path (Align Commodities estimate)

Rather than a fixed probability grid, we frame a path view driven by the tug-of-war. The key questions: does "surplus gravity" or the "low-stock shield" win — and which tail does the Sept–Oct flowering light?

Q3 (Jul–Sep): surplus gravity wins — a stair-step lower

As the harvest peaks and withholding eases, spot begins to flow and the surplus narrative pulls price down. But the low-stock shield prevents a crash, making a measured stair-step decline (working band 230–270¢) the base path — a high-volatility grind, with sharp retracements on every rain/harvest-delay/geopolitical headline.

Q4 (Oct–Dec): flowering and EUDR fork the road

Two events fork the path. Benign flowering (good rain) dissolves 27/28 fears, surplus gravity resumes, and the lower band (working 210–240¢) gets tested. Disrupted flowering (heat/drought) ignites 27/28 fears and, with EUDR-start premium, opens a 290¢+ upside tail. A capitulation flush of accumulated withholding could also open a brief sub-200¢ downside tail.

Path summary: the central H2 path is a high-volatility stair-step lower, but the absolute floor is structurally high (low stocks, costs), as our November piece argued. The three pivots that fork direction: ① a Jul–Sep bottom in Cecafe exports/ICE stocks ② Minas Sept–Oct flowering rain ③ the EUDR 12.30 enforce/delay decision. Surplus presses the "level"; flowering lights the "tail."

Implications & Strategy — Updating the November Calls

We revise November's strategy guidance in light of H1's outcome.

Re-grading the November advice

  • "Pre-secure 60–70% of H1 needs in Q1" → bought the top. Price fell all quarter, so early bulk pre-buying hurt. Update: in H2, with a stair-step-lower base, scaled, trailing buying is superior — in a surplus, time favors the buyer.
  • "Buy 330¢ support / sell 400¢ resistance" → support broke. 330¢ gave way. Update: shift from static support-buying to dynamic levels driven by stock/export data.
  • "Calendar spread (Long Mar/Short Jul) — backwardation" → directionally right. Backwardation held all H1. Update: while withholding and low stocks persist, the backwardation bet beats flat long on risk-reward — keep.
  • "Straddle for La Niña volatility" → the event came as El Niño. The regime was wrong but "buy volatility" was right. Update: in H2, use OTM calls/straddles aimed at the Sept–Oct flowering to own cheap asymmetric upside.

Roasters · Importers (H2)

  • Trailing scale-in + flowering calls: run scaled buying across 230–270¢ on a trailing basis, covering Sept–Oct upside with OTM calls.
  • Prepare for EUDR bifurcation: secure traceable lots ahead of the 12.30 start, phased so a delay leaves little sunk cost.
  • Diversify origin/grade: hedge both Brazil-withholding and surplus scenarios with Colombian washed and Vietnamese robusta.

Traders · Hedge Funds (H2)

  • Surplus trend + flowering hedge: the trend is down, but overlay shorts with OTM calls into the Sept–Oct flowering to defend tail risk.
  • Backwardation/calendar: bet on spread easing, triggered by a stock bottom and export recovery.
  • Event volatility: concentrate vol-buying around the EUDR 12.30 decision and the flowering peak.

Key monitors

  • Cecafe monthly exports · ICE stocks — first gauge of surplus realization / withholding release
  • Minas Sept–Oct rain & temps — the 27/28 upside-tail pivot
  • EUDR simplification/delay news — the two-way regulatory variable
  • USD/BRL 5.10–5.30 · Hormuz · tariff suit — macro/geopolitics
  • Consumer stocks · robusta substitution — the quiet demand downside

Conclusion

Seven months ago we framed 2026 as "order within uncertainty" and put the shift to a bearish regime at the center. That structural call was right — Brazil delivered a record crop, the market turned to surplus after four deficits, and the floor held above 200¢. But the two path variables — price level and climate — missed: reality was a lower price (243.9¢) and the opposite climate (El Niño). You can be right on direction and still wrong on level and path — that is H1's lesson.

H2 sits on a "surplus gravity vs low-stock shield" structure, where the Sept–Oct flowering lights the tail and EUDR 12.30 raises a regulatory cliff. The central path is a high-volatility stair-step lower, but the absolute floor is structurally high. The key question is no longer "will the bumper crop lower price" — that is answered — but "how far does surplus press the level, and which tail does flowering light?" And this time, we are not betting on a single climate regime.
