Weekly Market Summary

Coffee futures rebounded off the prior week's 19-month low to close the week net higher. July arabica (KCN26) slid to ~243.90c on Tuesday (6/9), its lowest since November 2024, but rallied Wednesday and Thursday to recover a one-week high of ~253.45c, before easing ~1.05c (-0.38%) on Friday (6/12) to settle near 252.40c. Against the prior Friday close (246.50c), that is a weekly gain of ~+5.90c (+2.39%), clawing back part of the prior week's -7.2% slide.

The direct trigger was a reversal in the weather narrative. The "dry weather, faster harvest" story that had pressured prices flipped to "rain, harvest delay" after forecaster Vaisala called for moderate-to-heavy rainfall across Brazil's coffee belt, potentially extending into next week. The Japan Meteorological Agency (JMA) officially confirmed El Nino in the equatorial Pacific on Wednesday, pushing the September-October flowering risk to the front of market attention. Safras & Mercado reported Brazil's 2025/26 harvest at 35% complete as of June 11 (vs 37% a year ago; five-year average 35%), confirming fieldwork is tracking a normal pace despite the rain.

Robusta (RMN26) also rallied hard on Thursday, up +109 (+3.25%) to ~$3,463/MT. ICE certified arabica stocks fell further to a 6.5-month low of 402,709 bags on Wednesday (6/10), a 2.5-month declining streak that kept spot tightness supportive. Meanwhile the real strengthened from USD/BRL 5.18 toward ~5.10, breaking out of the prior week's weak range and lending side support by reducing Brazilian farmers' incentive to sell.

Key dynamic of the week: The same Brazil-weather variable that produced last week's -7.2% crash flipped sign again within a single week — from bearish (dry, faster harvest) to bullish (rain, harvest delay) — delivering a +2.4% rebound, reinforced by a stronger real (5.18→5.10) that blunted one of last week's bearish drivers. Yet the structural bearish picture (record Brazil crop, 9.5-10M-bag surplus) is intact, so the short-term drivers remain "harvest pace, weather headlines, and FX."

Fundamental Analysis

1. Weather — "Rain Delay" Momentum and El Nino Confirmation

The core driver of the rebound was the change in the weather forecast. Forecaster Vaisala said moderate-to-heavy rainfall is forecast across Brazil's coffee belt and could extend into next week. June rain during the harvest peak can delay fieldwork and affect drying and quality, triggering immediate short-covering in a market that had built a bearish "faster harvest" thesis. That said, as Safras & Mercado's 35% progress (a normal pace) shows, the harvest itself is largely on schedule — a short-lived support that could fade quickly if the rain proves temporary and fieldwork resumes.

JMA confirms El Nino (6/10): El Nino, previously framed as a probability (NOAA super-El Nino odds 67%), moved to the "fact" stage with the JMA's official confirmation. A delay in Sept-Oct Brazil flowering rains would be negative for the 2027/28 crop — raising the realism of an asymmetric upside risk the current bumper-crop/surplus consensus is overlooking.

Despite this week's rebound, the medium-term balance of fundamentals still tilts to oversupply. The USDA FAS record Brazil 2026/27 forecast of 71.9M bags (+14%, arabica 47.5M +25%), Rabobank's upgraded global arabica surplus (7.0M→9.5M bags, incl. 5.9M arabica), and StoneX's 2026 global surplus of 10M bags (6-year high) anchor the bearish consensus. Consultancy forecasts from Marex (75.9M) and Sucafina (75.4M) also converge on records. Meanwhile Volcafe cut its 2025/26 Brazil arabica estimate to 34.4M bags on drought damage, leaving a temporal asymmetry — tight old crop (25/26) vs abundant new crop (26/27) — that underpins the backwardation.

4. Vietnam / Exports — Robusta Supply Pressure Persists

Per Vietnam's statistics office, Jan-May coffee exports rose +7.9% y/y to 922,000 MT. The USDA FAS projects Vietnam's 2025/26 output up +6.9% y/y to 31 million bags. Rising supply from the world's largest robusta producer is a structural drag on robusta prices, suggesting this week's robusta rebound (+3.25%) was driven more by arabica sympathy and short-covering than by improving fundamentals. On the Brazil side, Cecafe reported April green-coffee exports down -1.3% y/y to 2.76M bags.

5. CFTC Speculative Positioning

The latest confirmed reading (as of 6/2, released 6/5) shows managed money net long in Coffee C at 19,624 contracts (42,530 long minus 22,906 short). Net length has been partly rebuilt off the recent trough, meaning funds are not fully short. This implies two-way vulnerability: when weather headlines turn bullish, short-covering and momentum buying can amplify rebounds; when the harvest resumes, positions are exposed to liquidation. The new COT (as of 6/9, released 6/12) was not yet reflected in aggregated data at the time of writing, so this week's positioning shift needs confirmation next week (single-source / pending update).

6. Macro — Real Strengthens, El Nino

USD/BRL: ~5.10 on June 12, a shift to a stronger real from the prior week's 5.18 weak range. A firmer real lowers Brazilian farmers' selling incentive (in real-denominated proceeds), curbing the release of withheld supply and lending support to this week's rebound. That said, the US tariff proposal on Brazil and US-Iran negotiation uncertainty remain two-way FX risks (FX level is single-source).

El Nino: JMA confirmation plus NOAA super-El Nino odds of 67%. Sept-Oct Brazil flowering is the first watershed. Normal flowering rains would extend the bearish 27/28 bumper-crop narrative; a delay would push asymmetric upside risk into prices from late Q3. Brazil's late-June to July frost season is a parallel monitoring item.

Implications

1. A weather-headline tape — absence of directional conviction: The same Brazil-weather variable flipping sign two weeks running (-7.2% then +2.4%) and whipsawing prices both ways shows a market in "equilibrium-seeking" mode near the 19-month low — structural bearishness largely priced, but no conviction in further downside. Expect a high-volatility range hypersensitive to harvest pace, rain forecasts, stocks, and FX headlines.

2. El Nino from "probability" to "fact" — asymmetric risk made real: The JMA confirmation upgrades the overlooked Sept-Oct flowering risk from tail risk to a base-case variable. In a bumper-crop/surplus consensus, the value of call options and upside hedges structurally rises; combined with the lower vol premium after the sell-off, this creates a cheap asymmetric hedge opportunity.

3. A stronger real — the rebound's new ally: In last week's report a weak real (5.18) was a bearish driver stoking farmer selling; this week's move to 5.10 blunted that force. A further move toward 5.0 would reinforce farmer withholding and support the rebound, whereas a reversal back above 5.20 could revive selling pressure — making FX the key short-term swing factor.

4. Twin lows in stocks and price persist: Stocks at a 6.5-month low with price at a 19-month low means the market is heavily pre-pricing new-crop inflows while physical remains tight. A harvest or logistics disruption could trigger a spot squeeze and a sharp backwardation widening — which buffered the downside during this week's rebound.

5. Roasters / importers — reactivating scaled hedges: The rebound from the 243c washout to 252c offers a chance to execute part of the upper-mid band of a 230-260c scaled-buy program. Given the structural surplus, chasing the full size is risky; pairing with call-option coverage for El Nino / rain optionality is the sensible approach.

Market Outlook & Risks

Short-Term Structure (1-3 months)

The trend is still down, but this week's rebound re-confirmed a near-term floor around 243c. First resistance is 255-256c, then 262-265c (prior-week highs). Support sits at 247c, then 243c → 240c on a break. While rain momentum and a firmer real hold, a retest of 255c is possible, but until 263c is reclaimed all rebounds are best read as countertrend bounces within a bear market.

Technical view: On the July daily, a rebound off Tuesday's 19-month low (243.90c), with Friday a mild pullback from the one-week high. Holding 247c support and clearing 255c is the near-term rudder. Backwardation persists — spot tightness coexisting with futures weakness. High dependence on rain / El Nino / FX headlines keeps volatility elevated.

Medium-Term Outlook (3-12 months)

Brazil's harvest moves into its July-September peak (currently 35% done), bringing new-crop inflows in earnest. If the large surplus jointly projected by USDA, Rabobank, and StoneX (9.5-10M bags) materializes, further downside pressure dominates into Q4 2026. However, three buffers temper the pace of decline: (1) historically low stocks, (2) the Sept-Oct flowering risk from the JMA-confirmed El Nino, and (3) farmer withholding amplified by the firmer real. The base case is therefore "stair-step lower with intermittent weather- and FX-driven spikes" rather than a crash.

Key Risk Factors

  • Brazil harvest / rain (biggest short-term swing): Sustained rain → harvest delay → further rebound. Return to dry weather and resumed harvest (35% → faster) → rebound fades and prices fall again. Two-way, high volatility.
  • El Nino / frost (Sept-Oct; late-June-July; upside): JMA-confirmed, NOAA super-El Nino 67%. A flowering-rain delay or a frost event would make the 27/28 crop an asymmetric upside risk.
  • Brazilian real (two-way swing factor): Strengthening from 5.10 toward 5.0 reinforces farmer withholding and supports the rebound; a reversal above 5.20 accelerates selling and offsets it.
  • Speculative positioning (two-way): Managed money net long 19,624 contracts (6/2). Next week's COT will show this week's rebound impact — accelerating short-covering is upside, re-liquidation is downside.
  • Spot squeeze (upside): With stocks at a 6.5-month low plus farmer withholding, a harvest or logistics disruption could sharply widen the backwardation.
  • Structural supply (downside): Record Brazil crop, 9.5-10M-bag surplus, rising Vietnam exports — the root driver of medium-term bearishness is unchanged.

Overall Analysis

This was a week of a weather- and FX-driven technical rebound within a structurally bearish cycle. KCN26 rebounded from Tuesday's 19-month low (243.90c) to ~253.45c on Thursday and, despite Friday's mild pullback, settled near 252.40c for a weekly gain of +2.39%, recovering part of the prior week's -7.2% plunge. The drivers were Vaisala's Brazil rain forecast (harvest delay), the JMA's El Nino confirmation, and a shift to a firmer real (5.18→5.10). ICE stocks at a 6.5-month low supported the downside in the background.

The key is the separation of structure (bearish) from short-term momentum (rebound). The medium-term picture — record Brazil crop and a 9.5-10M-bag surplus — is unchanged, yet with bearish conviction thin near the 19-month low, +2.4% moved on weather and FX headlines. Notably, the real — last week's bearish driver — turned supportive, shifting the weight of the short-term swing factor toward FX, while El Nino's move from "probability" to "fact" builds upside optionality into the flowering season.

Trading view in brief: A 243-263c range with elevated volatility. 247c support and 255c resistance are the first watershed; the bearish structure holds until 263c is reclaimed. Key monitors: (1) sustained Brazil rain and harvest progress (Vaisala / Safras / Somar), (2) ICE stock trajectory, (3) next week's COT (this week's rebound impact), (4) USD/BRL 5.0-5.20, (5) El Nino / frost / Sept-Oct flowering updates.

For roasters and importers, executing the upper-mid of a 230-260c scaled-buy band after the 243c washout, paired with call-option-based asymmetric hedges made cheaper by the El Nino confirmation, is the sensible course. The supply outlook is heavy, but stocks are light, the weather risk has become real, and the real has turned stronger — until this asymmetric mix resolves, one-way bets warrant caution.
