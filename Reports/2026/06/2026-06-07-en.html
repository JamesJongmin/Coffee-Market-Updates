This Week at a Glance

Coffee futures fell throughout the week and closed at a fresh 19-month low after the USDA's official confirmation of a record Brazil crop. July arabica (KCN26) settled Friday (6/5) at 246.50¢, down 19.10¢ (-7.19%) on the week from the prior Friday's 265.60¢. Prices opened the week soft at 262.75¢ on Monday, then accelerated lower after Wednesday's USDA FAS forecast of a record 71.9M-bag Brazil 2026/27 crop (+14% y/y) — falling -1.94% Thursday (247.15¢) and touching the lowest nearest-futures level since November 2024 on Friday.

Robusta (RMN26) also closed at a 7-week low of $3,316/MT (-1.07% Friday). A weaker Brazilian real — which slid to a 1.75-month low against the dollar (USD/BRL 5.18) — added pressure by encouraging export selling from Brazilian producers. Friday's loss was limited to -0.26%, however, as ICE-monitored arabica inventories fell to a 5.75-month low of 419,504 bags, keeping the spot market tight.

Key dynamic this week: What had been a private-consultancy consensus on a Brazilian bumper crop is now officially validated by the USDA. Prices are down -13.2% over the past four weeks, and the market's driver is shifting from forecasts to the actual pace of new-crop inflows.

News Briefing

  • (6/3, USDA FAS) Brazil Coffee Annual: 2026/27 production forecast at a record 71.9M bags (+14% y/y), arabica at 47.5M bags (+25%), and exports surging +30%. The report declares the end of five years of weak production — though exporters remain cautious given low stocks and El Niño uncertainty.
  • (6/5, Rabobank) Raised its 2026/27 global coffee surplus estimate from 7.0M to 9.5M bags (arabica-led), and per Comunicaffe sees arabica futures falling by roughly a third by Q4 2026.
  • (6/2, Vietnam GSO) Jan–May coffee exports of 922,000 MT (+7.9% y/y). Momentum slowed from +15.8% through April but remains on a record pace.
  • (mid-week, Somar Meteorologia) Minas Gerais received just 11.7–14.1mm of rain, 35–45% of the historical average — dry, hot conditions are keeping the harvest on track and accelerating fieldwork.
  • (Comunicaffe, single source) Speculators have withdrawn roughly $2.7 billion of long investments from coffee futures in 2026 — the largest liquidation in the ag/softs complex this year except soybeans.

Fundamentals

1. USDA confirmation — record Brazil 2026/27 crop

The week's decisive trigger was Wednesday's USDA FAS Brazil Coffee Annual, forecasting 2026/27 (Jul–Jun) production at a record 71.9M bags (+14% y/y). Arabica is seen at 47.5M bags (+25% y/y), near the record levels of 2018 and 2020, with exports forecast to surge +30% on the bumper harvest.

The USDA's 71.9M is more conservative than the top private estimates (75–76M) but sits 5.2M bags above Conab's official 66.7M. Reinforcing the surplus theme, Rabobank raised its 2026/27 global surplus estimate from 7.0M to 9.5M bags, alongside StoneX's 10M-bag projection — the distribution of forecasts continues to converge upward.

2. Brazil weather & harvest — dry conditions accelerate fieldwork

Somar Meteorologia reported weekly rainfall in Minas Gerais — Brazil's largest arabica state — of just 11.7–14.1mm, or 35–45% of the historical average. Dry, hot weather is speeding the harvest and new-crop flows, pressuring prices all week. At the same time, Rio Times and others report farmers are withholding sales for tax-planning reasons and in hopes of better prices — physical flows are not yet as aggressive as the crop numbers suggest.

ICE arabica certified stocks fell from 427,840 bags Wednesday to 419,504 Friday — a 5.75-month low, extending a 2.5-month downtrend. The unusual combination of falling stocks and falling prices amid a bumper-crop outlook shows old-crop supply remains tight ahead of new-crop arrivals, and was the key factor limiting Friday's decline.

4. Vietnam — exports still rising, momentum cooling

Vietnam's National Statistics Office reported Jan–May coffee exports of 922,000 MT, up +7.9% y/y. Momentum has cooled from the +15.8% pace through April, but shipments remain on a record trajectory after 2025's all-time-high 1.58 MMT (+17.5%). With 2025/26 production projected up +6% to a 4-year high of 1.76 MMT (29.4M bags), Vietnamese supply remains a structural headwind for robusta.

5. CFTC speculative positioning

This week's COT release (positions as of 6/2) could not be cross-verified across multiple sources by publication time, so we withhold specific figures. The last confirmed reading (as of 5/19) showed a non-commercial net long of +8,554 contracts, shrinking for a fourth straight week. Comunicaffe reports (single source) that speculators have withdrawn about $2.7 billion of long exposure from coffee futures in 2026 — given this week's 7% slide, further fund selling and liquidation are likely. Next week's COT data warrants close attention.

6. Macro backdrop — BRL reversal, Hormuz, El Niño

USD/BRL: The real closed Friday at 5.179, a 1.75-month low (down roughly -2.8% from 5.037 a week earlier). Real strength had supported farmer withholding; this reversal raises export-selling incentives and could draw held-back supply into the market.

Strait of Hormuz closure: Ongoing disruption continues to lift freight, insurance, fertilizer, and fuel costs for importers and roasters — a near-term price support that could evaporate quickly on any resolution.

NOAA El Niño outlook: NOAA maintains an 82% probability of El Niño emerging between May and July, with a 67% chance of a "Super El Niño." Delayed September–October flowering rains would threaten the 2027/28 crop — the USDA itself cited El Niño uncertainty as a reason exporters remain cautious.

7. Global supply-demand context (USDA FAS / ICO)

Per the USDA FAS December bi-annual report, 2025/26 world production is a record 178.848M bags (+2.0% y/y) — arabica 95.515M (-4.7%) versus robusta 83.333M (+10.9%). 2025/26 ending stocks are seen falling -5.4% to 20.148M bags, still historically low, and ICO data put 24/25 global exports at 138.658M bags (-0.3%). The FAS semi-annual update due in late June will formalize 2026/27 global numbers and sharpen the surplus debate.

Implications

1. From forecast to confirmation — the price driver shifts: The USDA's official numbers remove the uncertainty premium around the bumper crop. What moves the market now is not competing estimates but actual June–August harvest progress and port shipment data. Smooth harvesting means further downside; any disruption gets amplified by low stocks.

2. The paradox of falling stocks and falling prices: A 19-month price low at a 5.75-month inventory low means the market is aggressively front-running new-crop arrivals. With farmers withholding and stocks thin, any harvest or logistics hiccup leaves room for a spot squeeze — which is why backwardation persists.

3. The BRL reversal is the next trigger: USD/BRL moved from the low-5.0s to 5.18. If weakness extends beyond 5.30, withheld farmer supply could convert into FX-driven selling — a double supply impulse of bumper crop plus currency incentive. Whether USD/BRL holds above 5.20 is next week's key checkpoint.

4. Roasters/importers — shift the hedge band lower: At 246¢ — the cheapest since November 2024 — the prior 250–285¢ laddered-hedging band warrants a downward reset (e.g., 230–260¢). Rabobank's "another third lower by Q4 2026" scenario argues for patience, but with an 82% El Niño probability, multi-year-low stocks, and Hormuz still unresolved, waiting for the bottom with full size is risky. Staggered buying remains the rational approach.

5. Asymmetric hedges getting cheaper: The sell-off has further deflated out-of-the-money call premiums. For El Niño and logistics risks the market is not pricing, call-based asymmetric hedges now offer better value.

Outlook & Risks

Short-term structure (1–3 months)

The trend is decisively lower. Friday's fresh low (the 246¢ area) is first support; a break targets 240¢ and then the psychological 220¢ zone. On bounces, 255¢ and then the prior week's low area of 262–265¢ should cap. Friday's contained -0.26% loss reflected inventory tightness — an oversold bounce is possible, but it would not signal a trend change.

Technical view: Six consecutive weeks of weakness on the July daily chart. A break of 246¢ opens 240¢ then 220¢. All rallies are corrective until 263¢ is reclaimed. Backwardation persists — tight spot coexisting with weak futures.

Medium-term view (3–12 months)

Brazil's harvest enters its July–September peak, bringing new-crop volume to market. If the large surpluses projected by USDA, Rabobank, and StoneX (9.5–10M bags) materialize, downside pressure dominates into Q4 2026 — Rabobank sees arabica futures roughly a third lower by year-end. Historically low stocks and El Niño risk should act as brakes, making a stair-step decline the base case rather than a crash.

Key risk factors

  • Brazil harvest pace: Continued dry weather accelerates harvesting — bearish. A return of wet conditions delays fieldwork — a short-term bounce trigger.
  • El Niño (Sep–Oct): 82% NOAA probability. Delayed flowering rains would put 2027/28 at risk and could start pricing in from late Q3 — an asymmetric upside risk.
  • BRL: A hold above 5.20 accelerates farmer selling and amplifies downside; a return to 5.0 sustains withholding.
  • Strait of Hormuz: Resolution removes the logistics premium — bearish; escalation brings a sharp short-term rally.
  • Speculative positioning: COT data gap this week. The net long may have flipped to net short — confirmation could mark either an oversold-bounce setup or further liquidation.
  • Spot squeeze: With stocks at a 5.75-month low and farmers withholding, any harvest/logistics disruption could blow out backwardation.

Conclusion

This week the structural bear cycle moved from the private-forecast stage to USDA-confirmed reality. KCN26 plunged -7.19% to 246.50¢, the lowest close in 19 months, with Rabobank's surplus upgrade (9.5M bags) and the BRL's reversal accelerating the move. Yet ICE stocks at a 5.75-month low and farmer withholding show the market is not yet physically abundant.

The next phase is harvest realization: June–August harvest progress, port shipments, and whether withheld farmer supply is released (a function of the BRL) will set the second-half price path.

Trading takeaway: The near-term range shifts down to 240–263¢. A break of 246¢ risks 240¢ then 220¢; the bear structure holds until 263¢ is reclaimed. Key monitoring: ① Brazil harvest progress (Safras & Mercado) and port shipments ② ICE inventory trend ③ next week's COT print (this week's gap) ④ USD/BRL vs 5.20 ⑤ Hormuz talks ⑥ NOAA El Niño updates.

For roasters and importers, a hedge band reset to 230–260¢ with staggered buying — paired with cheap call options against El Niño and logistics risk — is the sensible play. The supply outlook is heavy but inventories are light; until that asymmetry resolves, one-way bets deserve caution.
