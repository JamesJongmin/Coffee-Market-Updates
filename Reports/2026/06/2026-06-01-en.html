This Week at a Glance

Coffee futures closed lower this week amid sharp volatility centered on Brazil’s weather. July arabica (KCN26) settled at 265.60¢, falling -3.15% (-8.65¢) on Friday alone. Tuesday May 26 marked an intraday 1.5-year nearest-futures low. Heavy rains in Brazil mid-week sparked harvest-delay concerns, lifting prices to 274.25¢ by Thursday, before updated forecasts for dry conditions next week allowed the resumption narrative to reassert itself on Friday.

Robusta (RMN26) also closed down -2.19% (-$78) on Friday. The weekly trajectory was a clear downtrend punctuated by weather-driven reversals. Arabica is down -6.99% over the past month and roughly -22% year-to-date — a substantial repricing.

Core dynamic: Single weather headlines now swing the market by ±3% or more. This reflects a market that has largely priced in the structurally bearish consensus around Brazil’s 2026/27 bumper crop, leaving harvest disruption as the lone near-term support variable. Bearish fundamentals dominate while volatility expands.

Fundamentals

1. Brazil 2026/27 bumper-crop consensus — global surplus largest in 6 years

The dominant driver this week was StoneX’s projection of a 10-million-bag global coffee surplus for 2026 — versus 1.8 million bags in 2025, a 5.5× increase and the largest surplus in 6 years. Multiple agencies’ Brazil forecasts reinforce the picture.

Conab’s official forecast of 66.7M bags (revised up from 66.2M in February) is the most conservative. Private agencies cluster in the 71–76M bag range. For arabica alone, Conab projects 45.8M (+28% y/y, third-largest on record), Hedgepoint 50.2M, Sucafina 49.7M. The combination of the on-year of the biennial cycle and favorable rainfall is the foundation. Rabobank stated that, absent a major weather event, the market will gradually enter a phase of large surplus.

2. Vietnam — surging exports pressure robusta

Rising shipments from the world’s top robusta producer continue to weigh on prices. Per Vietnam’s National Statistics Office, Jan–Apr 2026 coffee exports rose +15.8% y/y to 810,000 MT. Full-year 2025 exports hit a record 1.58 MMT (+17.5% y/y), and 2025/26 production is forecast +6% y/y to a 4-year high of 1.76 MMT (29.4M bags).

However, dryness concerns in Vietnam’s Central Highlands provide near-term support. Weather forecaster Vaisala noted that recent showers in the main growing region have been spotty and that more rain is needed to aid cherry growth.

3. ICE certified stocks — both arabica and robusta declining

Despite the bumper-crop consensus, ICE certified inventories have trended lower over the past two months. Arabica stocks fell to a 3.5-month low of 435,430 bags on Friday May 29; robusta inventories reached a 2-year low of 3,631 lots on May 15. The old-crop tightness ahead of new-crop arrival is the key near-term constraint preventing a freer fall in prices.

4. Brazil exports — April data stabilizes

Cecafé’s May 12 report shows April total coffee exports at 3.12 million bags, +0.6% y/y. Green coffee exports fell -1.3% to 2.76 million bags, with arabica green shipments at 2.26 million bags, -15.9% y/y. January-April cumulative shipments to Germany (-12.9%) and the US (-41.5%) fell sharply, while volumes to Italy (+3.2%) and Belgium (+15.4%) rose.

CFTC data as of May 19 (F&O combined) shows non-commercial net long at +8,554 contracts, narrowing by 4,004 contracts week-over-week. Longs fell by 1,190 and shorts rose by 2,814, reinforcing bearish positioning. Total open interest expanded by 14,821 to 234,834 contracts — suggesting fresh short flow rather than long liquidation. The bumper-crop consensus is now translating directly into fund short positioning.

6. Macro backdrop — BRL, Hormuz, El Niño

USD/BRL: Friday May 29 close at 5.0370, with the real maintaining modest strength. News of a preliminary US-Iran framework and strong Q1 Brazilian GDP (+1.1% q/q) have sustained real strength. A firmer real discourages export sales by Brazilian producers.

Strait of Hormuz closure: Continues to raise global shipping, insurance, fertilizer, and fuel costs, pressuring roasters and importers. A near-term price support — but one that could evaporate quickly if negotiations resolve.

NOAA El Niño outlook: NOAA estimates an 82% probability of El Niño conditions May–July and a 67% probability of a Super El Niño. El Niño can delay Brazil’s September-October flowering rains, posing a latent risk to the 2027/28 crop (not the 2026/27 cycle currently being harvested). The market has not yet priced this in meaningfully.

7. Global supply-demand context (USDA FAS / ICO)

The USDA FAS bi-annual report (December 18) projects 2025/26 global coffee production at a record 178.848 million bags (+2% y/y). Arabica declines -4.7% to 95.515M bags while robusta climbs +10.9% to 83.333M bags — an unusually asymmetric structure. Brazil 2025/26 is forecast at 63M bags (-3.1%); Vietnam at 30.8M (+6.2%, a 4-year high). 2025/26 ending stocks are projected to fall -5.4% to 20.148M bags, still historically low. ICO data show 2024/25 global exports at 138.658M bags, -0.3% y/y.

Implications

1. Structurally bearish, but stocks are low — a "slow normalization" scenario. Even if the 2026/27 bumper crop is confirmed, ICE certified stocks sitting at absolute lows and 2025/26 ending stocks projected -5.4% means new-crop arrival alone will not restore equilibrium. Full normalization likely requires 2–3 more seasons. A deep break below 200¢ is therefore unlikely.

2. Volatility expansion phase. The market swinging ±3% on single weather headlines (Brazil rains → dry forecast) is likely to intensify through the June-August harvest window. Traders should expect the trend-following short to be paired with active short-term volatility management as the dominant operational concern.

3. Roaster / importer perspective — hedging window opening. The 245-265¢ zone has not been visited since November 2024 and represents a meaningful discount versus 2024/25 average procurement prices. Rather than concentrated hedging at one level, laddered hedges across 250-285¢ are more appropriate. A break below 220¢ would open a deeper accumulation window.

4. Asymmetric upside risks — El Niño and Hormuz. The market has not priced NOAA’s 82% El Niño probability. A collapse of Hormuz negotiations, combined with already-depleted stocks, could push prices back above 300¢ on logistics shock alone. OTM call options have become relatively cheap and well-suited for asymmetric hedging strategies.

5. Robusta relative strength may persist. The combination of Vietnam Central Highlands dryness and ICE robusta stocks at a 2-year low argues for a continued narrowing of the arabica-robusta spread (robusta outperformance). Blenders and instant-coffee buyers should review formulation flexibility and revisit hedge ratios.

Outlook & Risks

Short-term structure (1–3 months)

The market sits in a dual structure: medium-term surplus expectations pulling prices lower, while low stocks, roaster bids, Hormuz, and BRL strength support the downside. Technically, the July contract is in a clear downtrend; Tuesday May 26’s 1.5-year low is the primary support. A break below this level risks a slide toward 220¢. Strong resistance is expected at 285¢.

Technical view: July contract daily chart shows a sustained downtrend. The May 26 low is the critical support. A recovery above 290¢ would suggest a trend change; otherwise expect 220-285¢ range trading. Backwardation persists but the spread is gradually narrowing.

Medium-term view (3–12 months)

The dominant view into H2 2026 is improving supply. Brazil’s 2026/27 harvest accelerates May-August and new-crop physical flow begins reaching the market. If StoneX’s 10M-bag surplus materializes, further downward pressure is likely through Q4 2026.

That said, most analysts expect material new-crop flow only from Q3 2026 onward. In the interim, a low-stock / short-term tightness phase may temporarily support prices.

Key risk factors

  • Brazil weather: June-August rainfall distribution during harvest is critical. Excessive humidity threatens quality and elevates mold/rust risk; persistent dryness accelerates harvest but can affect late crop development.
  • El Niño (Sep-Oct): At NOAA’s 82% probability, delayed flowering rains would compromise 2027/28 yields — a latent upside catalyst for late-H2 2026 prices.
  • Strait of Hormuz: A negotiated resolution would quickly remove the logistics premium → additional downside. Escalation would deliver sharp near-term spikes.
  • BRL: Strong Q1 GDP sustains real firmness. A 4.90-5.10 range maintains near-term export restraint; a break above 5.30 would accelerate Brazil exports and add bearish pressure.
  • Speculative volatility: Further narrowing of CFTC non-commercial net long would accelerate fund unwinding — yet over-accumulated shorts can itself become a technical bounce trigger.
  • Vietnam rainfall: Continued Central Highlands dryness raises 2026/27 robusta concerns and could pull arabica higher in sympathy.

Conclusion

The coffee market has entered a structurally bearish phase where the Brazil bumper-crop consensus is being meaningfully priced in. StoneX’s 10M-bag global surplus forecast and the 75-76M Brazil production range from multiple agencies pushed prices to a 1.5-year low. ICE certified stocks at absolute lows, the Hormuz closure, BRL strength, and latent El Niño risk all anchor the downside.

In the near term, the July contract is likely to range-trade with the May 26 low (~263¢) as primary support and 285¢ as resistance. June’s Brazil harvest progress and weather variability will be the key monitoring points.

Trading view (summary): Expect 250-285¢ near-term range. A break below the May 26 low (~263¢) opens downside toward 220¢. Resistance at 285¢ initially, with stronger resistance at 300¢. Key monitors: ① Brazil June rainfall and harvest pace; ② ICE inventory changes; ③ CFTC non-commercial positioning; ④ USD/BRL; ⑤ Hormuz negotiations; ⑥ NOAA El Niño updates.

For roasters and importers, 245-265¢ presents a reasonable laddered hedging opportunity, and call options remain attractive given asymmetric upside catalysts (El Niño, Hormuz). In a market where the bumper-crop consensus is strong but physical inventories are low, disciplined risk management remains paramount.
