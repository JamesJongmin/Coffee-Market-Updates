Executive Summary

The coffee market stands on two contradictory signals. On one side: a USDA-confirmed record Brazil crop (71.9M bags) and 19-month-low prices. On the other: an El Niño that NOAA assigns an 82% probability of emerging — with 67% odds of a "Super El Niño." The market has put all of its weight on the former.

This report dissects that asymmetry. Three conclusions. First, El Niño is unlikely to dent the 2026/27 headline surplus directly — Brazil's arabica is already being harvested, which is the rational core of the market's indifference. Second, the exposure lies elsewhere: Vietnam's robusta and Brazil's conilon (26/27, harvested from October), and Brazil's arabica 2027/28, which is decided by the August–October flowering window. Third, as the 2023–25 cycle showed, El Niño supply shocks move prices non-linearly in a low-inventory world — Vietnam's -20% crop was the starting point of the all-time high of 440.85¢.

One-line takeaway: The crop is a present fact; El Niño is a future probability. The market has priced the fact and ignored the probability — which is why calls are cheap, and why now is the window for asymmetric hedges.

Mechanism — How El Niño Moves Coffee

1. The physiology of flowering: why Sep–Oct rains decide everything

Brazilian arabica flowers en masse with the first substantial rains of August–October, as the dry season breaks. Flowering is an event of days, not months; moisture stress at that moment desiccates blossoms and fails fruit set. The pinhead-stage fruit that follows is equally sensitive to November–December rains. In short, the timing and distribution of September–October rainfall effectively caps the 2027/28 crop — and El Niño's signature effects in Brazil (delayed, irregular Southeast rainfall plus heat) target precisely that window.

2. The climate-change baseline: trees already weakened

Analyses cited by StoneX CoffeeNetwork show global coffee regions now endure roughly 47 extra days of harmful heat per year — about 57 days across top producers, and ~70 additional heat-stress days annually in Brazil alone. With flower desiccation, premature cherry development, erratic drying, and expanding pest ranges already routine, an El Niño lands not as an addition but as an amplifier. As CoffeeNetwork puts it: "Climate change sets the stage. ENSO plays the script."

1997/98 — intensity isn't everything

In the strongest El Niño on record, Brazilian arabica was largely spared — and at times even benefited. The damage concentrated in Colombia and Central America through heat stress and moisture deficits. Yet the price response was muted, because the world was in a supply glut driven by Brazil and Vietnam. Lesson: El Niño's price effect depends less on its strength than on the inventory backdrop. Today's backdrop — ICE stocks at a 5.75-month low, historically thin global ending stocks — is the opposite of 1997's.

2015/16 — direct hit on the robusta belt

The 2015/16 event brought drought to Vietnam and Indonesia, cutting global coffee production by roughly 10% in 2016. Brazilian arabica largely held, but conilon suffered — a cycle of violent arabica–robusta spread swings.

2023/24 — the nearest rehearsal

The El Niño that began in late 2023 delivered back-to-back drought and heatwaves to Vietnam's Central Highlands. Per Vicofa, the 2023/24 harvest fell -20% to 24.5M bags, among the smallest in a decade; Central Highlands robusta prices surged ~44% y/y, and 2024/25 failed to recover at ~22M bags. That supply shock, compounding into chronically low stocks, fed directly into February 2025's all-time arabica high of 440.85¢. Academic work (Ubilava 2012, on ENSO and coffee price dynamics) likewise finds arabica prices respond asymmetrically to El Niño phases.

Synthesis: A super El Niño (1997/98) couldn't move prices in a high-stock world; a merely "strong" one (2023/24) helped trigger all-time highs in a low-stock world. 2026's inventory backdrop resembles the latter.

Market Impact — Why the Market Ignores It, and Whether That's Right

1. The timing asymmetry: the 26/27 surplus is already "locked"

The indifference has a rational core. Brazil's 26/27 arabica is already being harvested — if El Niño develops now, harvest-season dryness actually helps fieldwork. El Niño therefore cannot easily unwind the 9.5–10M-bag surplus projections from USDA, Rabobank, and StoneX. That is why May's short-covering bounce faded quickly.

But the logic stops at two borders. ① Vietnam's 26/27 robusta, harvested from October — an intensified dry season can hit final cherry filling, with conilon in the same exposure zone. ② Brazil's 27/28, decided by Aug–Oct flowering — if flowering disruption becomes visible, the market must reprice an entirely different multi-year path: one bumper year followed by one deficit year.

2. Positioning: a match in a dry forest

Speculators have withdrawn roughly $2.7B of length YTD (Comunicaffe, single source), with the net position shrinking toward a possible net-short flip. Paradoxically, that builds fuel for short-covering to amplify any El Niño trigger — May's brief El Niño-driven rally was the preview.

3. Options: asymmetry has gotten cheap

Six straight weeks of decline have compressed out-of-the-money call premiums. Against 67% super-El Niño odds and a defined Sep–Oct risk window, Q4 2026–Q1 2027 call structures offer their best risk-reward of the year.

Scenario Analysis (Align Commodities estimates)

The probabilities below are our own estimates, combining NOAA's emergence (82%) and super-El Niño (67%) odds with the conditional probability that flowering rains are actually impaired. The crux: El Niño occurring ≠ crop damage — even a strong event is benign if September–October rains arrive on time.

Scenario 1: No event / weak El Niño (~20%)

ENSO stalls at neutral-to-weak and flowering rains are normal. The bumper-crop/surplus narrative remains the sole driver; Rabobank's path (arabica roughly another third lower by end-2026) plays out, with a test below 200¢ plausible. Robusta follows under new Vietnamese supply.

Scenario 2: Moderate-to-strong El Niño, flowering rains adequate (~40%)

El Niño develops but Brazil's Sep–Oct rains arrive without major delay. 27/28 worries produce intermittent short-covering rallies — higher volatility within a bearish trend, a 220–280¢ band. Robusta outperforms on Vietnam dry-season damage. Base case.

Scenario 3: Super El Niño + visible flowering disruption (~40%)

Niño3.4 tracks toward +2.5°C and delayed Minas rains plus heat are confirmed in observed data. The 27/28 outlook flips — surplus year followed by deficit re-entry — and the market reprices toward 300¢+. If Vietnamese drought compounds, the 2023–25 cycle re-runs: a robusta-led surge with short-covering as the amplifier, and low stocks making the move non-linear.

Price outlook summary: For the next ~3 months (harvest season) the path of least resistance stays lower in every scenario. The pivot is late August–October: the first flowering rains in Minas Gerais. Until that data prints, the market's indifference can persist; once it prints, the hedge is no longer cheap.

Risk Management

Roasters & importers

  • Staggered physical buying + call overlay: keep the 230–260¢ laddered-buying band, and use compressed premiums to add Q4 2026–Q1 2027 OTM calls (e.g., 280–300¢ strikes) against 27/28 risk. In the bumper-crop case, the cost is only the premium.
  • Audit robusta exposure: El Niño's first target is the robusta belt. Blends heavy in robusta warrant higher coverage ratios before Vietnam's October harvest.
  • Revisit quality clauses: the wet East Africa/Colombia scenario is a quality-and-logistics risk more than a volume risk — check spec and substitution clauses.

Producers & exporters

  • Prices sit at 19-month lows — rather than chasing sales, defined-risk put structures cover Scenario 1 while keeping the El Niño upside open.

Key monitoring indicators

  • NOAA monthly ENSO diagnostic (second Thursday) — emergence/strength probability path
  • Niño3.4 SST / RONI weeklies — tracking the ECMWF +2.5°C trajectory
  • Sep–Oct Minas Gerais rainfall (Somar, Climatempo) — timing and volume of the flowering trigger rains
  • Vietnam Central Highlands dry-season rainfall and reservoir levels — final 26/27 cherry filling
  • ICE certified stocks — the thickness of the shock-absorber
  • CFTC non-commercial net position — the short-covering fuel gauge (watch for a net-short flip)

Conclusion

The biggest risk in the 2026 coffee market is not the bumper crop — it is the probability being ignored behind it. NOAA's 82% emergence and 67% super-El Niño odds are among the loudest advance warnings a commodity market has received, and the target windows (Brazil's Aug–Oct flowering, Vietnam's dry season) are well defined. 1997 teaches that El Niño doesn't always lift prices; 2024 teaches that in a low-stock world, one origin's -20% can end at all-time highs. Today's inventory backdrop looks like 2024's.

Bearish inertia can persist through the next two to three months of harvest. But from late August, the market's gaze inevitably turns to the skies over Minas. Hedges are only cheap before it rains.
