Market Overview

시장 개요

According to Barchart data as of January 2, ICE arabica March futures (KCH26) closed at 357.30 cents, up +8.55 cents (+2.45%) from the previous session. ICE robusta March futures (RMH26) rose +5 cents (+0.13%). Coffee prices recovered from early losses on Friday and settled higher as strength in the Brazilian real sparked short covering in coffee futures.

Brazilian Real Strength as Key Driver: According to Barchart analyst Rich Asplund, the Brazilian real (USD/BRL) rallied to a 2-week high on Friday, discouraging export sales from Brazil's coffee producers and sparking short covering in coffee futures. The real's strength means Brazilian producers receive reduced dollar-denominated coffee sales revenue, leading them to delay export shipments.

Yahoo Finance reported that coffee prices climbed to 1.5-week highs on Monday, December 30, with Somar Meteorologia reporting that Brazil's largest arabica coffee-growing area, Minas Gerais, received 11.1 mm of rain during the week ended December 26, or 17% of the historical average. Tridge noted that a severe heatwave is forecast to persist in Brazil's key coffee-growing regions, amplifying market concerns.

According to Trading Economics, ICE-certified arabica stocks stood at 456,477 bags as of December 29, down 54% from 991,080 bags a year ago. Flooding in northern Sumatra, Indonesia has affected about a third of arabica coffee farms and could cut 2025-26 exports by up to 15%. The US 50% tariff on Brazilian coffee imports continues to exacerbate supply tightness.

Coffee prices in 2025 marked one of the most volatile and complex years in recent history due to erratic weather, low stocks, and production risks in major producing countries. Volatility is expected to persist in 2026 due to the market's sensitivity to Brazilian weather conditions, although mid-to-long-term global supply improvement is anticipated.

Supply Fundamentals

공급 펀더멘털

Brazil Heatwave and Drought Intensify: DTN Progressive Farmer (December 30) reported that Brazil continues to experience erratic and unpredictable weather patterns, characterized by drought, insufficient rainfall, and cold fronts that can lead to frost and hail in major producing areas. Dry conditions and possible heat stress on coffee trees in Minas Gerais are notable features, with arabica coffee production expected to decline significantly in the 2025/26 Brazilian harvest.

Barchart (December 30) reported that Somar Meteorologia indicated Minas Gerais, Brazil's largest arabica-growing region, received 11.1 mm of rain during the week ended December 26, representing just 17% of the historical average. Tridge (December 27) reported that a severe heatwave is forecast to persist in Brazil's key coffee-growing regions, amplifying market concerns.

2026/27 Production Forecasts - Contrasting Views: An Itaú BBA report cited by Trading Economics noted that the climate outlook for late December and early 2026 is positive, as crops are in the flowering stage and forecasted rains provide ideal conditions for this critical period of production. However, Brazilian production remains hampered by five years of climatic volatility, while the biennial cycle of the crop further restricts near-term supply growth.

According to Sucafina's latest market report, following their Brazil crop tour, they increased their 2026/27 production outlook to 49.7 million bags of arabica and 25.9 million bags of robusta. Both Cerrado and Rondonia are expected to produce record amounts of coffee, and weather conditions continue to be nearly ideal for all regions. However, pressure from this crop will likely only come in Q2 of 2026, but it could be significant in altering perceptions of future coffee supply.

Vietnam Export Records: Saigon Giai Phong (January 2) reported that Vietnam's 2025 coffee exports reached over 1.5 million tons worth $8.4 billion, marking a historic achievement. This represents a 16.9% increase in volume and 60.7% increase in value compared to 2024. Export prices averaged $5,600 per ton, sometimes reaching as high as $5,850 per ton. The EU and US are major markets, with emerging markets like Algeria and Mexico also showing significant growth.

According to Tuoi Tre News, during the 2024-25 crop year (October 2024 to September 2025), Vietnam exported over 1.5 million tons, with volume up 1.8% and value up 55.5%. VICOFA Chairman Nguyen Nam Hai forecasts that yields could increase by around 5-10% in the 2025-26 season, though prices may not remain as strong as the previous season.

Inventory & Supply-Demand

재고 및 수급

ICE Inventory Tightness Intensifies: Comunicaffe (December 31) reported that ICE-certified arabica stocks stood at 455,727 bags as of December 30, consisting mainly of coffee from Brazil (81,527 bags), Mexico (72,560 bags), Peru (69,077 bags), Tanzania (41,517 bags), and Honduras (37,781 bags). Most stocks (374,281 bags) are stored in warehouses at the port of Antwerp, Belgium.

According to Barchart, ICE arabica inventories fell to a 1.75-year low of 398,645 bags on November 20 before recovering to a 2-month high of 456,477 bags in mid-December. ICE robusta inventories fell to a 1-year low of 4,012 lots on December 10 before recovering to a 4-week high of 4,278 lots in mid-December, though they remain at tight levels.

US Tariff Impact Persists: According to Barchart, American buyers shunned Brazilian coffee purchases due to high tariffs on US imports from Brazil. US purchases of Brazilian coffee from August through October, during which President Trump's tariffs were in effect, dropped by 52% from the same period last year to 983,970 bags. Those US tariffs have since been cut, but US coffee inventories are still tight.

USDA Global Supply-Demand Outlook: The USDA Foreign Agriculture Service (FAS) bi-annual report released December 18 projected that world coffee production in 2025/26 will increase by +2.0% y/y to a record 178.848 million bags. Arabica production is forecast at 95.515 million bags (-4.7%), while robusta is expected at 83.333 million bags (+10.9%). Brazil's 2025/26 production is forecast at 63 million bags (-3.1% y/y), and Vietnam's at 30.8 million bags (+6.2% y/y, 4-year high). Ending stocks are forecast to fall by -5.4% to 20.148 million bags from 21.307 million bags in 2024/25.

Supply Outlook vs. Reality: According to Trading Economics, despite improving weather conditions, low inventories and stronger demand both domestic and external are keeping prices from falling sharply. Brazilian production remains hampered by five years of climatic volatility. The ICO reported on November 7 that global coffee exports for the current marketing year (Oct-Sep) fell -0.3% y/y to 138.658 million bags, suggesting tight supplies.

Vietnam Export Trends: According to Vietnam's National Statistics Office, November coffee exports jumped +39% y/y to 88,000 MT, with Jan-Nov cumulative exports rising +14.8% y/y to 1.398 MMT. Vietnam's 2025/26 coffee production is projected to climb +6% y/y to 1.76 MMT (29.4 million bags), a 4-year high. VICOFA said that Vietnam's coffee output in 2025/26 will be 10% higher than the previous crop year if weather conditions remain favorable.

Analysis & Outlook

The coffee market surged to 357.30¢ in the first week of the new year, showing strong upward momentum. While extreme drought and heatwave forecasts in Brazil's Minas Gerais region amplify near-term supply concerns, Itaú BBA's positive rainfall outlook and Sucafina's 2026/27 production upgrade support mid-to-long-term supply improvement expectations.

Vietnam's record export performance ($8.4B) demonstrates the sustainability of high price levels, but Sucafina's noted Q2 2026 supply pressure implies potential downside price correction risk. Near-term volatility is expected to expand in the 350-370¢ range.
