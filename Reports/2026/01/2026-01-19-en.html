Weekly Market Summary

Market Overview

Coffee markets experienced a modest pullback this week as profit-taking emerged following updated weather forecasts calling for rain in Brazil's coffee-growing regions. March Arabica (KCH26) settled at 355.30 cents on Friday, reversing from an intraday high of 361.30 cents.

Key Issue: Prices rallied near 1-month highs through Thursday but reversed Friday as updated forecasts increased the probability of rainfall in Brazil's coffee regions over the coming week. Minas Gerais received only 29% of historical average rainfall for the week ending January 9, but weekend rain expectations triggered profit-taking.

Markets remain in a structurally tight supply situation. ICE certified Arabica stocks stand at 462,000 bags, down 54% from year-ago levels (991,000 bags). While stocks have recovered to a 2.5-month high, they remain near historically low levels.

On the geopolitical front, the US seizure of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro raised tensions with Colombia and Brazil—top Arabica producers—though the practical impact on supply chains appears limited.

Brazil Weather Update

Minas Gerais Conditions

Rainfall in Minas Gerais, Brazil's largest arabica-growing region, remains well below average. According to Somar Meteorologia, precipitation for the week ending January 9 totaled just 26.5mm, or 29% of the historical average—worse than the 38.3mm (76% of average) recorded for the week ending December 19.

However, weather forecasters are calling for rain to return to Brazil's coffee regions starting this weekend. Vaisala expects isolated showers across parts of Brazil over the coming days, which should support the 2026/27 crop currently in the bean formation phase.

Itaú BBA has characterized the late December/early January weather outlook as positive, with forecasted rains providing ideal conditions for crops in the flowering stage. However, Sucafina warns that supply pressure may materialize in Q2 2026, projecting Brazil's 2026/27 arabica output at 49.7 million bags.

Global Supply Dynamics

Production & Trade

Vietnam achieved record coffee exports in the 2024/25 crop year. According to VICOFA, export value reached $8.4 billion, up 60.7% year-over-year. Export volume exceeded 1.5 million tons (+6% y/y), with average export prices at $5,610/ton (+52.7% y/y).

Vietnam Export Record: 2024/25 crop year (Oct-Sep) exports hit $8.4B, volume exceeded 1.5M tons. 2025/26 production forecast at 30.8-31M bags (+6-10% y/y). Maintains status as world's largest robusta supplier.

Indonesia suffered severe damage from Cyclone Senyar in late November, with floods and landslides affecting approximately one-third of arabica farms in northern Sumatra. According to the chairman of the Association of Indonesian Coffee Exporters and Industry (AICE), 2025-26 exports could decline by up to 15%. Indonesia is the world's third-largest robusta producer; the disaster claimed 1,157 lives and displaced over 380,000 people.

Brazil's 2026/27 crop outlook is increasingly optimistic. StoneX forecasts production at 70.7 million bags, up 13.5% year-over-year and a potential record. Arabica is projected at 47.2M bags (+29.3%), robusta at 23.5M bags (-8.9%). Safras & Mercado is also projecting 71 million bags.

US Tariff Policy Update

Brazil Tariff Removal

US tariffs on Brazilian coffee have been fully removed. President Trump signed an executive order on November 20 exempting Brazilian agricultural products from the 40% additional tariff imposed in August. This followed the November 14 exemption of the 10% reciprocal tariff on coffee and other products not grown in the US.

Tariff Timeline: July - 50% tariff imposed (10% reciprocal + 40% national emergency) → November 14 - 10% reciprocal tariff exempted → November 20 - 40% additional tariff fully removed. US purchases of Brazilian coffee during Aug-Oct fell 52% y/y to 984,000 bags.

Coffee futures plunged on the tariff removal news. On November 21, arabica fell more than 6% intraday to 2-month lows. However, European traders suggested prices are unlikely to fall below $3/lb given the global arabica deficit, low exchange stocks, and La Niña-related supply risks.

National Coffee Association President Bill Murray welcomed the decision: "Two-thirds of American adults drink coffee each day, and every cup will cost less thanks to President Trump's decision to remove tariffs on coffee imports from Brazil—the world's largest coffee producer." Thousands of bags of Brazilian coffee held in bonded warehouses are expected to move quickly to US roasters.

Medium-term, StoneX and Safras forecasts of a 70-71M bag Brazil 2026/27 crop support supply improvement expectations. However, low ICE stocks and weather uncertainty should limit downside near-term. Range: 350-365 cents expected.
