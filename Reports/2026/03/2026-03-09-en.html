Weekly Summary

The coffee futures market reversed last week's sharp decline, rebounding on the back of an extraordinary geopolitical shock: the Strait of Hormuz blockade triggered by the Iran war. May arabica settled Friday at 293.30¢, up +12.55¢ (+4.47%) week-over-week, hitting a 3-week high. May ICE robusta (RMK26) also rallied to a 2.5-week high.

Following coordinated US-Israeli strikes on Iranian military targets on February 28, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) formally declared the Strait of Hormuz closed on March 2, threatening to target any vessel attempting transit. The effective shutdown of a waterway through which approximately 20% of the world's oil passes has sent global shipping rates, insurance premiums, and fuel costs surging — raising costs for coffee importers and roasters worldwide.

⚠ Geopolitical Alert: Vessel crossings through the Strait of Hormuz collapsed to fewer than 5 per day as of March 4 — a roughly 90% decline from normal levels. Maersk, CMA CGM, and Hapag-Lloyd have suspended all transits. War risk insurance premiums surged 5x from 0.25% to 1.25% of vessel value. Approximately 3,200 ships, roughly 4% of global tonnage, are idle in the Persian Gulf. President Trump proposed US Navy escorts, but traffic normalization remains elusive.

Midweek volatility was amplified when the Brazilian real fell to a 1.5-month low against the dollar on Tuesday, sparking long liquidation in coffee futures as a weaker real encourages Brazilian export sales. The weekly pattern featured a tug-of-war between geopolitical supply disruption concerns (bullish) and Brazil's bumper crop outlook combined with real weakness (bearish).

Key Dynamic: The market is caught between a 'near-term geopolitical supply disruption premium' and a 'medium-term Brazilian bumper crop surplus outlook.' The bearish trend that prevailed through late February has been temporarily interrupted by the Hormuz crisis, and the direction of the conflict will likely determine the market's near-term trajectory.

Fundamental Analysis

1. The Strait of Hormuz Crisis & Coffee Market Impact

The military conflict initiated by US-Israeli strikes on Iran ('Operation Epic Fury') on February 28 has created unprecedented disruption to global maritime logistics. The Strait of Hormuz is the world's most critical maritime chokepoint, handling approximately 20% of global seaborne oil and a significant share of LNG shipments.

While coffee does not transit directly through the Strait of Hormuz, the crisis impacts the coffee market through several transmission channels. First, the global shipping network disruption reduces overall vessel availability and raises freight rates across all routes. Second, Houthi-controlled Yemen has resumed attacks on commercial shipping in the Red Sea and Bab el-Mandeb strait, forcing Suez Canal traffic to reroute around Africa's Cape of Good Hope — adding weeks and significant cost to Europe-bound coffee shipments.

The surge in crude oil prices (Brent above $82/bbl, up 13% since the conflict began) further compounds shipping costs through higher fuel expenses. The transmission pathway of energy price surge → freight cost increase → rising coffee import costs is acting as a clear price-supportive factor.

2. Brazil: Export Decline Continues, Bumper Crop Outlook Intact

Brazil's Secretariat of Foreign Trade (SECEX) reported that February coffee exports declined -16.2% y/y to approximately 2.502 million bags. January exports had already fallen -30.8% y/y to 2.78 million bags. Cumulative exports for the 2025/26 crop year (July–January) totaled 23.406 million bags, down -22.5% y/y in volume, though up +8.1% in revenue terms.

The export decline is largely attributed to the backwardation structure in futures, which discourages forward selling by producers. According to Safras & Mercados, pre-sales of Brazil's 2026/27 crop have reached only ~8% of production potential — well below the historical average of 17%.

Meanwhile, the 2026/27 bumper crop outlook remains firmly intact. Estimates range from Conab's official 66.2M bags to Sucafina's 75.6M bags, with all institutions projecting record output. Sucafina's crop tour concluded with increased estimates, projecting record production from both the Cerrado and Rondonia regions. Weather has been cooperative: Somar Meteorologia reported Minas Gerais received 78mm of rainfall in the week ending February 20, or 131% of the historical average, supporting the grain-filling stage.

3. Colombia: February Production Plunges -36%

Supply concerns from the world's second-largest arabica producer continue to deepen. The National Federation of Coffee Growers (FNC) reported February production fell -36% y/y to 869,000 bags — following January's -34% decline to 893,000 bags.

February exports also fell sharply, down -32% y/y to 807,000 bags. Cumulative exports in the first five months of the current coffee year (October–February) totaled 5.06 million bags, a -14% decline. The FNC attributed the downturn to persistent excessive rainfall disrupting flowering and fruit development. The trailing 12-month production total (March 2025–February 2026) fell to 12.72 million bags, down -14% y/y.

This compounds near-term arabica supply concerns. Colombia is the world's largest supplier of washed arabica, a critical origin for US and European markets. The sharp reversal from 2025's 30-year production high underscores the sector's vulnerability to weather variability.

4. Vietnam: Export Surge Continues

The world's largest robusta producer continues to post extraordinary export growth. Vietnam Customs data shows January exports surged +56.4% y/y to 224,308 tonnes, with export value reaching $1.08 billion (+39.5% y/y). The 2025 full-year exports set a new record at 1.59 million tonnes valued at $8.92 billion (+58.8% y/y).

The Vietnam Coffee and Cocoa Association (VICOFA) projects 2025/26 production to rise approximately +10% y/y. Abundant robusta supply is the primary downside driver for robusta prices, and indirectly pressures arabica through substitution dynamics.

ICE arabica certified stocks reached 540,867 bags on Friday — a 5-month high, up approximately 36% from the 1.75-year low of 396,513 bags recorded on November 18. CFTC data as of February 24 shows the non-commercial net long position essentially unchanged at +12,814 contracts. Both longs (+819) and shorts (+865) increased, reflecting growing two-way participation. Open interest rose +7,594 to 164,239 contracts, suggesting fresh capital inflows. Spread positions expanded by +4,487 contracts, indicating active arbitrage activity.

The previous weeks' aggressive speculative liquidation appears to have stabilized. The OI increase and spread expansion signal renewed market engagement, possibly driven by the Hormuz crisis introducing a new risk premium into positioning.

7. Other Notable Developments

Brazilian Real Volatility: The real fell to a 1.5-month low against the dollar on Tuesday, triggering long liquidation in coffee. A weaker real incentivizes Brazilian producer export sales, increasing supply pressure. However, the oil price surge and broader risk-off sentiment created mixed currency dynamics later in the week.

Minas Gerais Rainfall: Somar Meteorologia reported 78mm of rainfall (131% of the historical average) in the week ending February 20, supporting favorable grain-filling conditions for the 2026/27 crop.

Dollar Strength: The US Dollar Index ($DXY) rose above 98.5 midweek, capping upside in dollar-denominated commodities including coffee.

Market Outlook & Risks

Near-Term Market Structure

The prevailing downtrend through late February has been interrupted by the Hormuz crisis — an exogenous shock. Technically, support has been confirmed around 280¢, with RSI and stochastic indicators showing bullish divergence signals. However, the medium-term downtrend has not been structurally reversed, and strong technical resistance exists at 300¢.

Technical Perspective: May arabica maintains a daily Sell signal but 280¢ support is confirmed, creating near-term rebound momentum. Positioned in the lower portion of the 52-week range (275.35–437.95¢). Backwardation persists (Mar–May spread at +4.30¢). A sustained Hormuz disruption could drive a test of 300¢ resistance.

Supply Disruption Scenario Analysis

The trajectory of the Hormuz crisis presents three key scenarios for the coffee market:

Scenario 1: Early Resolution (25% probability) — Diplomatic breakthrough leads to shipping normalization within 2–3 weeks. Coffee prices shed their geopolitical premium and return to a 270–285¢ range as Brazilian bumper crop fundamentals reassert dominance.

Scenario 2: Prolonged Disruption (50% probability) — The blockade persists for weeks to months. Structurally higher freight and insurance costs become embedded in coffee import pricing. Range widens to 280–310¢ with elevated volatility. Europe-bound coffee shipments face 2–3 week delays. Roasters accelerate inventory building.

Scenario 3: Escalation (25% probability) — Military conflict broadens, paralyzing maritime logistics across the Middle East. Crude oil surpasses $100/bbl; stagflation concerns spread globally. Coffee prices could reach 310–340¢, though recessionary demand destruction would cap the upside.

Medium-Term Outlook

Setting aside the geopolitical variable, the medium-term fundamental outlook remains bearish. Brazil's 2026/27 harvest beginning in May–June will materialize supply pressure. Rabobank's projected 8.64M bag surplus for 2026/27 and Sucafina's estimates of 49.7M arabica and 25.9M robusta bags point to significant downward price pressure through Q3–Q4.

However, Colombia's consecutive production collapses (-34% to -36%) serve as a stark reminder of arabica supply fragility. The -14% decline in trailing 12-month production reflects more than base effects — persistent excessive rainfall has damaged flowering cycles and may continue to impact output for several months.

Risk Factors

  • Strait of Hormuz Crisis: The dominant near-term uncertainty. A prolonged blockade would structurally raise global logistics costs and energy prices, transmitting upward pressure to coffee via dual channels. The effectiveness of US Navy escorts and Iran's escalation posture are key variables.
  • Brazil Weather: Currently favorable, but March–April rainfall patterns during the final crop development phase remain the critical determinant. Excessive humidity raises coffee rust (Hemileia vastatrix) risk.
  • Colombia Production: Two consecutive months of -34% to -36% declines. Whether March marks a recovery or continuation will be significant for near-term arabica supply-demand balance.
  • Brazilian Real: Elevated volatility. Weakness encourages exports (near-term bearish for coffee); strength suppresses exports (near-term bullish).
  • Global Macro: Middle East crisis-driven crude oil surge, re-emerging inflation concerns, and dollar strength all interacting simultaneously. Stagflationary scenarios could trigger demand destruction.

Conclusion

This week marked the onset of a fundamental-versus-geopolitical tug-of-war in the coffee market. Despite bearish fundamentals from Brazil's record crop outlook and recovering ICE inventories, the Strait of Hormuz blockade triggered by the Iran war has delivered an unprecedented shock to global logistics and energy markets, propelling coffee prices approximately 4.5% higher week-over-week.

Near-term, support at 280¢ has been reconfirmed, and the geopolitical premium is enabling a test of 300¢ resistance. However, this premium could dissipate rapidly upon any resolution of the Hormuz crisis, at which point Brazilian bumper crop fundamentals would reassert their dominance.

Trading Perspective: Near-term range of 280–310¢ expected. Upside risk to 310–340¢ if the Hormuz crisis escalates. Downside support at 275¢. Key monitoring points: ① Hormuz shipping normalization timeline, ② global freight and insurance rate trajectory, ③ March CFTC positioning data, ④ Brazil March–April rainfall patterns, ⑤ Colombia March production figures.

For coffee importers and roasters, the freight cost surge represents a direct impact on landed costs. Proactive measures to address potential shipping delays on European-routed volumes and scenario-based hedging strategies are recommended. The intersection of a historically low-inventory environment with an active geopolitical crisis warrants heightened risk management vigilance.
