Executive Summary

Thirty-five days have passed since Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) effectively blockaded the Strait of Hormuz on February 28, 2026, in retaliation for the US-Israeli strikes on Iranian nuclear and military facilities (Operation Epic Fury). The closure of this strategic waterway—through which approximately 20% of the world's oil and 20% of LNG transits—has sent shockwaves far beyond energy markets, delivering a structural blow to the entire global commodity logistics system.

Coffee does not directly transit the Strait of Hormuz. However, the blockade's impact has permeated every stage of the coffee supply chain through a cascading transmission mechanism: energy price surge → freight rate explosion → insurance premium spikes → Suez Canal avoidance → extended transit times. This report systematically analyzes these multi-layered effects and their implications for the global coffee industry.

Key Assessment: The Hormuz crisis is not a temporary logistics disruption but a factor that is structurally delaying the market's transition from the 2023–2025 supply deficit cycle to the anticipated 2026 surplus cycle. Despite Brazil's record crop forecast (75.9M bags), logistics bottlenecks are constraining the physical flow of supply into the market, slowing the timing and pace of price declines.

Multi-Layered Supply Chain Impact

1. Energy Price Channel: Direct Cost Escalation

The Strait of Hormuz is the world's largest energy chokepoint, handling approximately 20–25% of global seaborne oil trade. Since the blockade, Brent crude has surged roughly 60% from ~$78/bbl to $126/bbl. This directly translates into higher bunker fuel costs, significantly increasing the Bunker Adjustment Factor (BAF)—a core component of ocean freight rates.

Beyond transportation, energy price increases cascade into fertilizer costs (natural gas-based nitrogen fertilizers), drying and processing energy expenses, and domestic inland transport. In Brazil, trucking costs from Minas Gerais to the port of Santos have reportedly risen 10–15% due to diesel price increases.

2. Ocean Freight Explosion: Structural Cost Increases

According to Drewry's World Container Index, freight rates on major container routes have surged since the Hormuz blockade. This reflects not only higher energy costs but also a reduction in effective vessel capacity and route diversions.

Many vessels on this route previously transited the Suez Canal, and security concerns plus insurance costs near the Persian Gulf have forced a Cape of Good Hope reroute. Per Maersk, this adds 12–21 days to transit times, meaning the same fleet can handle fewer annual voyages.

3. War Risk Premiums: The Hidden Cost

According to Lloyd's List, war risk insurance premiums for waters near the Persian Gulf have surged from 0.05–0.1% to 0.5–1.0% of hull value—approximately a 4–5x increase. For large container vessels (hull value $150–200M), single-voyage premiums have skyrocketed to $750K–$2M, costs ultimately passed through to shippers.

Estimated logistics cost impact per coffee container (20ft FCL):

Vietnam→Europe: Pre-crisis total logistics $4,500–5,500 → Current $8,500–10,500 (~+90%)

Brazil→South Korea: Pre-crisis total logistics $3,500–4,500 → Current $5,500–7,000 (~+55%)

Converted to a per-bag (60kg) basis, the freight cost increase equates to approximately 3–8¢/lb—about 1–3% on top of the current arabica futures price (295¢/lb).

4. Route Diversions and Extended Transit Times

The most tangible indirect effect of the Hormuz blockade is the Cape of Good Hope rerouting of Suez-transiting vessels. Even vessels not directly passing through Hormuz are avoiding the Suez Canal due to security concerns and insurance cost spikes in nearby waters.

Extended transit times go beyond simple "delays"—they reduce the inventory turnover velocity of the entire supply chain. Fewer voyages per vessel per year means effective global shipping capacity declines, feeding back into higher freight rates in a self-reinforcing cycle. Drewry estimates the Cape reroute has reduced global effective container capacity by approximately 12–15%.

2. Brazil: The Paradox of Abundance

Brazil's primary export routes (Santos→Europe, Santos→Asia) have limited direct exposure to Hormuz. However, indirect effects are substantial.

The Bumper Crop Paradox: Marex forecasts Brazil's 2026/27 coffee production at a record 75.9M bags. Yet for this historic supply to exert its full bearish pressure on prices, the harvested coffee must move smoothly from origin to destination. The current global logistics crisis is structurally impeding this process.

  • Inland transport costs rising: Diesel price increases have pushed Minas Gerais→Santos trucking costs up 10–15%. Further escalation likely during harvest season (May–June) volume concentration.
  • Santos port congestion risk: Global vessel shortages are increasing demurrage times at Santos. Risk of severe port congestion when harvest volumes peak.
  • Producer selling holdback: Per Safras & Mercados, forward sales for the 2026/27 crop stand at just 8% of production potential (vs. 17% historical average). Logistics uncertainty is making producers even more cautious about forward commitments.
  • Fertilizer supply risk: The Hormuz blockade has disrupted Middle Eastern natural gas supply, driving up nitrogen fertilizer prices—a medium-term risk factor for the 2027 crop.

3. East Africa: Double Impact

Ethiopia, Kenya, and Tanzania face a double impact. Their coffee exports primarily route through Djibouti and Mombasa via the Suez Canal to Europe—now disrupted. Simultaneously, these energy-import-dependent nations face surging domestic fuel costs, raising inland transport and processing expenses. Specialty coffee producers in Ethiopia's Sidama and Yirgacheffe regions report that elevated shipping costs are eroding premium margins.

1. Arabica Futures: Bumper Crop vs. Logistics Risk Deadlock

ICE arabica May futures have traded in a wide 280–320¢ range since the Hormuz crisis. After hitting a 7-week high of 319.50¢ on March 24, prices corrected to the 295¢ level as Brazil's record crop forecasts were reaffirmed.

An estimated 10–15¢/lb "Hormuz premium" is currently embedded in market prices, inferred from the gap between pre-crisis levels (~285¢) and current prices (295¢), and the fact that prices haven't returned to pre-crisis levels despite even stronger bearish fundamentals from Brazil.

2. CFTC Positioning: Speculative Capital Pivot

Per CFTC data (as of March 24), non-commercial net long positions surged to +25,424 contracts, up 36.7% from +18,594 the prior week. This follows February's massive long liquidation (~$2.7 billion), suggesting the Hormuz risk premium is drawing speculative capital back into coffee.

Positioning Interpretation: The expansion of speculative net longs contradicts fundamentals (bumper crop = bearish). This likely reflects: (1) bets on prolonged Hormuz disruption, (2) expectations of freight cost pass-through to prices, and (3) volatility trading around near-term supply disruptions. However, any Hormuz resolution could trigger rapid position unwinding, accelerating price declines.

4. Robusta: More Direct Exposure

ICE robusta (London) is more directly impacted. With the primary robusta export route (Vietnam/Indonesia→Europe) requiring Suez transit, ICE robusta certified stocks have fallen to 4,093 lots—a 3.5-month low. This contrasts sharply with arabica certified stocks at 585,621 bags (6.25-month high), as arabica supply from Brazil and Central America flows through routes largely outside Hormuz's impact zone. The crisis is widening the supply gap between arabica and robusta.

Scenario Analysis and Outlook

Scenario 1: Early Resolution (Probability 20%)

Conditions: Diplomatic resolution or partial Hormuz reopening by April–May

Market Impact: Brent crude drops to $85–95/bbl. Ocean freight normalizes 50%+ within 4–6 weeks. Arabica May falls to 265–280¢. Brazil bumper crop effect fully reflected. Rapid speculative long liquidation expected.

Caveat: Per NPR, post-reopening mine clearance, insurance normalization, and fleet redeployment will take months. Full supply chain normalization requires an additional 3–6 months.

Scenario 2: Prolonged Stalemate (Probability 55%)

Conditions: Blockade persists through Q2–Q3 2026 with only partial shipping resumption

Market Impact: Brent crude in $110–130/bbl range. Ocean freight at current levels or marginally higher. Arabica trades in 280–310¢ range. Brazil harvest (May–June) supply gradually increases downward pressure, but logistics bottlenecks limit decline pace.

Key Variables: Brazil harvest pace, Santos port congestion, further robusta stock declines, energy price trajectory.

Scenario 3: Crisis Escalation (Probability 25%)

Conditions: Military escalation, additional chokepoint blockades (Bab el-Mandeb, etc.)

Market Impact: Brent crude $150+/bbl. Global shipping capacity –25%+. Arabica retests 320–380¢. Logistics costs add 10–15¢/lb. Robusta supply crisis intensifies, arabica-robusta arbitrage dynamics shift dramatically.

Second-Order Effects: Fertilizer price spikes threaten 2027 crop. Demand destruction accelerates. Global recession risk rises.

Risk Management Strategies

Recommendations for Importers & Roasters

  1. Reassess inventory strategy: Consider expanding safety stock from the typical 4–6 weeks to 8–12 weeks. Particularly for Vietnamese and East African origins, additional buffer for transit delays is essential.
  2. Diversify sourcing: Increase allocation to origins outside the Hormuz impact zone (Brazil, Colombia, Central America). However, quality profile changes and blending recipe adjustments must account for consumer response.
  3. Extend freight contracts: To mitigate spot rate volatility, consider 6–12 month service contracts with carriers. Negotiating leverage with shipping lines is key.
  4. Adjust hedging strategies: Despite the medium-term bearish outlook from Brazil's bumper crop, consider short-term put options given Hormuz risk. Scale-in buying within the 280–310¢ range remains viable.
  5. Strengthen logistics monitoring: Track Hormuz situation, Brent crude, major route freight rates, and insurance premiums on a daily basis. Develop contingency plans for escalation triggers (Bab el-Mandeb blockade, Brent $140+).

Conclusion

The Strait of Hormuz blockade is exerting an indirect yet structural and pervasive impact on the coffee market. The quadruple shock of energy price surges, freight rate explosions, insurance premium spikes, and route diversions has dramatically increased coffee logistics costs, particularly disrupting Southeast Asian robusta supply to Europe.

Brazil's record crop forecast (75.9M bags) remains a firm medium-term bearish factor. However, the Hormuz crisis is structurally delaying the physical market absorption of this bumper crop, pushing back the timeline for the market's anticipated transition to a surplus cycle.

Key Message: The coffee market is caught between two opposing forces—medium-term bearishness from record supply expectations and near-term cost escalation from geopolitical logistics disruption. This structure will be resolved by the fate of the Strait of Hormuz, and the timing and manner of that resolution will critically determine the trajectory of coffee prices in H2 2026.

For global coffee industry participants, this is a moment requiring balance between short-term risk management and medium-term opportunity capture. Defensive measures against rising logistics costs (inventory expansion, origin diversification, extended freight contracts) must be paired with preparation for strategic buying opportunities as Brazil's bumper crop eventually pressures prices lower.
