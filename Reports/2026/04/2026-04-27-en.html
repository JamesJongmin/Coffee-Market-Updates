Two-Week Market Recap (Apr 13–24)

Over the past two weeks, the coffee futures market alternated daily between geopolitical shocks and bumper-crop fundamentals, producing pronounced volatility. July arabica (KCN26) settled at 294.90¢ on April 24, finishing roughly 5–6¢ above the previous weekly report's reference point (~289¢ by the close of the week of April 13). In the interim, daily moves reached ±5%, with the period high near 306.90¢ and low near 279.45¢ — an amplitude of approximately 9.7%.

The dominant driver was the repeated change in Strait of Hormuz transit status. After Iran announced a temporary reopening on Friday, April 17, July arabica fell 4.7% over April 16-17 to 284.25¢ (a 10-day low). On Saturday, April 18, Iran reversed course and re-closed the strait, citing the continued U.S. blockade of Iranian ports — and the following week traded in an entirely different pattern. After registering a 7-week low of 279.45¢ on April 21, the contract rebounded +4.64% on April 22 and +3.87% on April 23, reclaiming a 4-week high.

Diagnosis: the market has not committed to a directional trend. Medium-term bumper-crop forecasts (StoneX 75.3M, Marex 75.9M, Sucafina 75.4M, Rabobank global 180M bags) form a strong downward force, while the Hormuz blockade, real strength, dryness in Minas, and a 16-month low in robusta stocks provide near-term support — confining prices to a 280–325¢ range. Daily volatility is currently overwhelming any underlying fundamental trend.

The two-week pattern can be summarized as two iterations of the "fundamental selling → geopolitical buying" cycle. Selling pressure surfaced whenever a Hormuz reopening looked plausible; buying pressure returned whenever closure was reaffirmed — leaving headline-reaction trading in control rather than any sustained trend. Baker Hughes announced on April 24 that its financial guidance assumes the Strait of Hormuz will not fully reopen until the second half of 2026, and a Dallas Fed Energy Survey found that roughly 80% of respondents expect reopening only in August or later.

Fundamental Analysis

1. Brazil: Bumper-Crop Consensus Forms Around 75M+ Bags

The dominant bearish factor over the past two weeks has been the convergence of consensus 2026/27 Brazilian coffee production forecasts above 75 million bags. Multiple analytical houses have upgraded their record-territory estimates.

On the back of these forecasts, StoneX projects a global 2026 surplus of 10 million bags — roughly 5.5× the 1.8M bags estimated for 2025 and the largest surplus in six years. Rabobank similarly projects a record 180 million bags of global 2026/27 production with a 7–10 million bag surplus, expecting arabica to settle in the 250–300¢ range by year-end.

A roughly 10-million-bag gap remains between the Brazilian government agency Conab's 66.2M estimate and the 75M+ figures from private houses. The discrepancy reflects differing views on how quickly drought-stressed trees are recovering. Actual yields will only become clearer once the harvest accelerates in May and the picture firms in June–July.

2. Brazilian Rainfall Deficit: Near-Term Support

Weather is the one variable that could meaningfully revise the bumper-crop outlook. According to Somar Meteorologia, Minas Gerais received just 4.2mm of rain — only 20% of the historical average — during the week ended April 20. This is a further deterioration from the prior week (11.7mm, 47%).

That said, per DTN, Minas Gerais accumulated 400–600mm of rainfall between late December and late February — leaving soil moisture in good shape. With the crop already in late-stage maturation, most analysts judge the late-April dryness to be of limited consequence for final yields. The market reads it as "not severely damaging, but supportive enough to sustain a price premium".

3. Strait of Hormuz: The Largest Source of Volatility

Status since April 18: Iran announced a 10-day reopening on April 17, then re-closed the strait the following day, citing the unresolved U.S. blockade of Iranian ports. On April 22, the IRGC seized two container ships transiting "without authorization", effectively halting commercial traffic almost entirely. CNBC reports only 8 vessels transited on April 22 (vs. the pre-war daily average around 100).

The direct impact of the closure is elevated freight, insurance, and fuel costs. Carlos Mera, Rabobank's head of agricultural commodities market research, noted on April 22 that rising oil prices feed directly into agricultural transport costs, with the decline in certified stocks amplifying that effect. StoneX's Daryl Kryst added that coffee beans are heavily reliant on fuel for transport from inland farms to ports, making prices highly sensitive to fuel costs.

Baker Hughes' April 24 Q1 earnings call disclosed financial guidance that assumes the Strait of Hormuz will not fully reopen until the second half of 2026. CEO Lorenzo Simonelli described geopolitical risk as having become "a structural reality" for oil and LNG markets and expects "persistent risk premiums". A Dallas Fed Energy Survey of roughly 100 oil and gas executives found that approximately 80% expect reopening only in August or later, while more than 80% see future disruptions as somewhat or very likely.

ICE arabica certified stocks stood at 527,866 bags as of April 17 — about a third below year-ago levels but more than 20% higher than at the start of the year. The recovery has been driven primarily by Honduran coffee passing grading (136,062 bags, the leading origin).

By contrast, ICE robusta certified stocks fell to a 16-month low of 3,755 lots on April 22. This is a key support for robusta prices — and one of the reasons May robusta (RMK26) surged +4.32% to $3,539 on April 23. That said, Vietnam's aggressive export pace (+14% y/y in Q1 2026) suggests inventory rebuilding could accelerate once logistics normalize.

5. Vietnam: Q1 Exports +14% y/y

Vietnam, the world's largest robusta producer, reported Q1 2026 (Jan–Mar) coffee exports of 585,000 MT, up +14% y/y. Full-year 2025 exports rose +17.5% y/y to 1.58 MMT (about 26.33 million bags). 2025/26 output is forecast at a 4-year high of 1.76 MMT (29.4 million bags), up +6% y/y.

Vietnamese wholesale prices have stabilized around 87,000–88,000 VND/kg in April — well below the late-2025 peak of 120,000 VND/kg, but still leaving comfortable producer margins, suggesting continued export flow.

6. Brazil Exports: Q1 -21.2% y/y

According to Cecafe's April 14 release, Brazil's Q1 2026 coffee exports totaled 8.4 million bags, down -21.2% y/y. Export revenue fell -13.6% y/y to USD 3.371 billion. March alone was down -10% y/y on a Cecafe basis (2.65 million bags); the Trade Ministry reported a -31% y/y decline (151,000 MT).

Cecafe president Márcio Ferreira attributed the weakness to (1) buyers awaiting the new crop, (2) port logistics problems, and (3) the international environment (Hormuz, real strength). Cecafe expects conilon/robusta shipments to recover starting in May, and arabica from July.

CFTC futures-only data (as of April 14) shows non-commercial long positions at 51,766 contracts (+1,623 w/w), shorts at 28,949 (+513), and net long at 22,817 contracts (+1,110 w/w). Open interest fell 7,577 contracts to 168,563.

The net long has rebuilt from a low of 12,860 contracts in late February to 22,817 by mid-April, reflecting the combination of the March–April Hormuz closure, real strength, and Minas dryness concerns. Liquidation pressure briefly appeared around the April 16-17 Hormuz reopening headlines, so the April 21 report (due next Friday) may show a more conservative reading.

Outlook & Risks

Near-Term Market Structure

July arabica (KCN26) is currently trading inside a 280–325¢ range, with headline-reaction trading expected to continue dominating any directional trend in the near term. Technically, the +8%-plus rally on April 22–23 broke above both the 100-day and 200-day moving averages, but the -1.81% pullback on April 24 has weakened that momentum.

Key levels: immediate support near 296¢ / first support 280¢ / April 21 low 279.45¢. First resistance 320–325¢ / strong resistance 350¢. Working trade range 280–325¢. A break of the lower bound risks an extension to 262¢; a break of the upper bound opens up a test of 350¢.

Price Drivers — Two-Week Recap (Ranked by Impact)

Ranked by influence over the past two weeks:

  • Strait of Hormuz transit status (largest variable): reopening/re-closure headlines have produced ±5% daily moves. The market's perception of "risk premium" has mattered more than the actual rise in freight and insurance costs.
  • Brazilian real: the 2-year high reached on April 22–23 was the principal upside trigger. A stronger real reduces Brazilian growers' dollar-denominated export incentives, tightening near-term supply.
  • Bumper-crop consensus: the 75M+ consensus has firmly capped the upside, with consistent profit-taking emerging near the 4-week high (306–310¢).
  • ICE robusta stocks at a 16-month low: direct near-term support for robusta and partial spillover support for arabica.
  • Brazil rainfall deficit: 4.2mm (20% of average) looks threatening, but ample soil moisture and the imminent harvest limit the actual yield impact.

Medium-Term Outlook

The pace of Brazil's new-crop harvest, which begins in earnest in May, will set the tone for the next 3–4 months. Rabobank projects arabica settling within a 250–300¢ range by Q4 2026; Cecafe expects conilon/robusta shipments to recover from May and arabica from July.

Yet the Hormuz variable looks unlikely to disappear quickly. Baker Hughes' guidance (assuming no full reopening until H2) and the Dallas Fed Energy Survey (80% expecting reopening in August or later) suggest the geopolitical premium could be structurally embedded for several more months. In that case, the tug-of-war between bumper-crop fundamentals and the geopolitical premium will likely extend beyond June.

Risk Factors

  • Hormuz normalization timing: a successful peace negotiation that fully reopens the strait could trigger a -10 to -15% near-term drop. Conversely, fresh military escalation could send prices +10 to +15% higher.
  • Brazilian new-crop shipment pace (May–June): grower hold-back behavior is the wildcard. Continued real strength could further delay shipments.
  • Frost risk in Minas (May–July): some analysts (e.g. Helena Coffee) identify cold-front frost as the largest near-term tail risk. A confirmed frost event could push prices +20% or more.
  • CFTC position liquidation: partial unwinding of the 22,817-contract net long would create downside pressure. Watch next Friday's release covering the April 21 data.
  • Roaster hedging behavior: active U.S. roaster buying has been observed near 280¢. Further weakness should attract hedging demand and support the lower bound.

Conclusion

Over the past two weeks the coffee market has traded in a "bearish fundamentals, bullish headlines" structure, range-bound throughout. Bumper-crop consensus (StoneX, Marex, Sucafina 75M+; Conab 66.2M) imposes clear downward pressure, but the Hormuz cycle, the 2-year high in the Brazilian real, Minas dryness, and the 16-month low in ICE robusta stocks together support prices above 280¢.

KCN26 closed at 294.90¢, near the middle of the range — almost exactly the midpoint between the 4-week high (~306.90¢) and the 7-week low (279.45¢). From this position, a breakout in either direction could deliver an asymmetric move, making this a moment to favor risk-managed hedging strategies over directional trading.

Trading view: expect range trading between 280–325¢ in the near term. A break below the lower bound risks 262¢; a break above could open up 350¢. Monitor: (1) Hormuz transit headlines, (2) Brazilian real, (3) weekly Minas Gerais rainfall reports, (4) CFTC data covering April 21 (released next Friday), (5) the pace of Brazil's new-crop shipments beginning mid-May.

Over the medium term, Brazil's new-crop arrivals (May–July) should pull prices into a 250–300¢ range; however, if the Hormuz crisis persists into August or beyond, the geopolitical premium could provide a firm floor. The reasonable stance is to trust the bumper-crop fundamentals while maintaining short-term hedges against geopolitical headlines.
