Weekly Market Summary

This week, the coffee futures market traced a V-shaped price pattern as bumper crop forecasts collided with currency dynamics. Through midweek, arabica slid to a 3-week low as Brazil's record-crop outlook dominated sentiment. Late in the week, however, the Brazilian real surged to a 2-year high against the dollar, triggering short covering that pushed the market back up. May arabica settled Friday (Apr 10) at 300.10¢, up 2.18% on the day and recovering a 1-week high.

Robusta painted a different picture. The nearest-futures contract hit an 8.5-month low midweek under pressure from surging Vietnamese exports, yet ICE robusta certified stocks simultaneously collapsed to a 1.25-year low of 3,977 lots, exposing acute near-term tightness. The divergence is stark: while arabica stocks recovered to a 6.25-month high of 585,621 bags, robusta inventories continue to shrink.

Core Issue: The market is moving along two separate time horizons. StoneX's April 6 forecast of record 182.5M-bag global production and Brazil's bumper crop outlook form one axis (medium-term structural bearishness), while robusta stock depletion, real strength, the Strait of Hormuz closure, and European stocks at a 2-year low form another (near-term support). The collision is widening volatility.

Fundamental Analysis

1. Brazil: Private Analysts Keep Revising Higher

Expectations of a record 2026/27 Brazilian crop dominated the market again this week. Private forecasts keep climbing well above Conab's official 66.2M bags projection.

On March 19, Marex Group projected Brazil's 2026/27 coffee crop at 75.9 million bags, even higher than Sucafina's 75.4M figure. StoneX raised its estimate to 75.3M bags on March 12 and held that number in its April 6 update, confirming its view that the 2026/27 harvest will jump 20.8% year-over-year. The common thread: every major forecast sees an all-time record.

Critically, StoneX projects the 2026 global coffee surplus will expand to 10 million bags, up from 1.8M in 2025 — the largest surplus in six years. Brazilian stocks alone are expected to grow by roughly 5M bags, while global inventories are seen rising from about 38M to over 48M bags by end-2026.

Brazilian weather has sent somewhat mixed signals recently. Somar Meteorologia reported that Minas Gerais received 11.7mm of rain last week, just 47% of the historical average. However, from late December through end-February, Minas Gerais received 400-600mm of cumulative rainfall, keeping overall crop conditions in good shape. Brazil's robusta harvest is already underway, and the arabica harvest is set to peak from May-June onward.

2. Global Supply Outlook: StoneX 182.5M Bags — A Record

According to Perfect Daily Grind's April 10 industry review, StoneX now projects 2026/27 global production at a record 182.5 million bags. This exceeds Rabobank's prior 180M estimate. Brazil (+20.8% y/y) leads the growth, followed by a roughly +10% gain in Vietnam and a +3.6% rise in African output led by Uganda and Ivory Coast.

Breakdown of StoneX 182.5M Forecast: Brazil's +20.8% surge is the main driver. Vietnam builds on 2025/26 output of 1.76M MT (+6%) with an additional +10% in 2026/27. Africa contributes +3.6% via robusta expansion in Uganda and Ivory Coast. However, the report cautions that "despite expectations for price stabilization toward year-end, global stock replenishment is likely to remain uneven" — regions with slower stock recovery may face near-term tightness.

3. European & US Stocks: At 2-Year Lows

According to Perfect Daily Grind's April 10 report, European green coffee stocks have fallen to 2-year lows. Stocks at major European ports continue to trend lower per European Coffee Federation data. The US picture is even more dramatic: US green coffee stocks have plunged from over 6 million bags in 2022 to fewer than 1 million in 2025, with only modest recovery expected in 2026.

While this appears to contradict StoneX's surplus outlook, it actually reflects a structural shift — consumer-country stocks are depleting while producer-country stocks are accumulating. Tariff shocks, logistics delays, and backwardation-driven reluctance to forward-sell are blocking the normal flow of beans from origin to destination. This gap is likely to close once Brazil's 2026/27 new crop begins flowing through ports in Q2-Q3.

4. Vietnam: Q1 2026 Exports +14% y/y

Surging exports from Vietnam, the world's largest robusta producer, remain a key bearish factor for robusta prices. According to Vietnam's National Statistics Office, January-March 2026 coffee exports rose 14% y/y to 585,000 MT. Full-year 2025 exports jumped 17.5% y/y to 1.58 MMT. Vietnam's 2025/26 production is projected to climb +6% y/y to a four-year high of 1.76 MMT (29.4 million bags).

ICE-monitored arabica inventories rose to 585,621 bags, a 6.25-month high as of March 18. The rebuild reflects Brazilian semi-washed physical prices approaching tenderable parity, which has driven continued stock inflows. In contrast, ICE robusta stocks fell to a 1.25-year low of 3,977 lots as of April 10. Despite Vietnam's export surge, certified stocks keep shrinking — a paradox that reflects strong direct-trade physical demand and limited volumes meeting ICE quality specifications.

According to the CFTC Legacy Report (futures-only, as of March 31), non-commercial speculators held a net position of -65,117 contracts (net short). The notable development: short positions dropped by 43,161 contracts, indicating heavy short covering. Longs also fell by 12,474 contracts, while total open interest contracted by 13,169 to 927,493 contracts.

This signals that the excessive speculative short positioning built up through late March is now unwinding. The unwind reflects a convergence of near-term supportive factors — the real surge, collapsing ICE robusta stocks, and the Strait of Hormuz closure — which together triggered Friday's short-covering rally. With residual shorts still above 257,000 contracts, further covering remains possible.

7. Strait of Hormuz Closure: Logistics Risk Persists

Supply Chain Risk: The closure of the Strait of Hormuz continues to disrupt global shipping and strain coffee supply chains. Freight rates, insurance, and fuel costs are all higher, weighing on importers and roasters. European-bound Vietnamese robusta and Indonesian arabica routes face the most disruption. European roasters' rush to secure stock — combined with European inventories at 2-year lows — is providing near-term price support.

8. Brazilian Exports Collapse

Recent Brazilian monthly exports have fallen more sharply than expected. Per Cecafe, February green coffee exports dropped 27% y/y to 2.3 million bags, while Brazil's Trade Ministry reported March coffee exports down 31% y/y at 151,000 MT.

Several factors explain the weakness. First, the backwardation structure — with futures below spot — is discouraging forward sales. Per Safras & Mercados, Brazil's 2026/27 crop is only about 8% pre-sold, below 9% a year earlier and well under the 17% historical average. Second, real strength reduces export incentives. Third, the low 2025 production base is still feeding through. These factors offer near-term price support, but the built-up inventory could hit the market in a wave once the 2026/27 new crop arrives in Q2.

Outlook & Risks

Near-Term Market Structure

The market is currently caught between medium-term surplus expectations (StoneX 182.5M global, Brazil +20.8% y/y) and near-term tightness (ICE robusta at 1.25-year lows, European stocks at 2-year lows, US stocks collapsed, real at a 2-year high, Hormuz closure, weak Brazilian exports). Reclaiming the 300¢ level in May arabica was a key psychological resistance, and Friday's break above it signals improving near-term momentum.

Technical View: Rebounded from weekly low around 289¢ (3-week low) back to 300¢. Near-term resistance at 305-310¢, key support at 290¢. Further unwinding of the large CFTC short position (257,848 contracts) could trigger upside momentum. The medium-term structure, however, remains biased bearish.

Demand-Side Dynamics

Roaster positioning is mixed. Major roasters are reporting higher green bean cost pressure, and sustained high prices are showing early signs of demand destruction in developed markets. Emerging markets — particularly China and Southeast Asia — continue to grow. Notably, European roasters are front-loading stock purchases given the Hormuz closure and 2-year-low inventories, which is actually strengthening near-term physical demand.

Industry consolidation is another variable worth watching. The April 1 completion of Hartree Partners' Touton SAS acquisition — following its 2025 Volcafe purchase — is accelerating concentration among top-tier coffee traders. Procurement volume and pricing power are shifting to a smaller number of large trading houses, a structural change that carries implications for market liquidity and volatility.

Medium-Term Outlook (Q2-Q4 2026)

The view for H2 2026 remains dominated by improving supply. As Brazil's 2026/27 harvest ramps up from May-June, physical supply pressure will intensify. StoneX projects a 10M-bag global surplus for 2026, pointing to accelerated stock replenishment, while Rabobank sees room for further arabica price adjustment through Q4 2026.

That said, as Perfect Daily Grind noted, "stock replenishment is likely to remain uneven." The low starting point for European and US consumer-country inventories keeps the market vulnerable to further weather shocks. The next major inflection point will be weather conditions during the Southern Hemisphere 2027/28 flowering window (October 2026 - March 2027).

Key Risks

  • Brazilian Weather: Minas Gerais recently received only 47% of average rainfall. Rainfall distribution through April during final crop development is critical. Excessive rain could also delay harvest and degrade quality.
  • Brazilian Real: At a 2-year high vs USD. Further strength continues to curb Brazilian exports, supporting prices near-term. A reversal would likely trigger renewed producer selling.
  • Strait of Hormuz: Prolonged closure would further lift logistics costs on European- and Asian-bound routes. Combined with 2-year-low European stocks, roasters are front-loading procurement.
  • Robusta Stocks: At a 1.25-year low of 3,977 lots. Further declines could trigger a robusta squeeze. Arabica-robusta spread risk is elevated.
  • Speculative Volatility: CFTC non-commercial net short of -65,117 contracts. Accelerated short covering could lead to near-term upside overshoots. New short positioning could resume downside pressure.
  • Industry M&A: The Hartree/Touton deal accelerates trading industry consolidation. Greater volume control by large houses is a new source of near-term volatility.
  • Consumer Country Stocks: US stocks collapsed from 6M+ bags in 2022 to under 1M in 2025. Potential supply-demand instability before Brazilian new crop flows.

Conclusion

This week's coffee market saw StoneX's record 182.5M-bag global production forecast — a structural bearish signal — squared off against near-term supports from the 2-year-high real, 2-year-low European stocks, and depleted robusta inventories. The 3-week low early in the week followed by Friday's short-covering recovery through the 300¢ psychological resistance demonstrates that the market's two-way sensitivity remains elevated.

In the medium term, price direction will be reconfirmed once Brazil's record harvest becomes physical reality in Q2 2026. The successive upward revisions from Marex (75.9M), Sucafina (75.4M), and StoneX (75.3M) confirm the market's transition into a "surplus regime." But with low consumer stocks, robusta tightness, currency and political variables, and ongoing industry M&A, the path is unlikely to be one-directional — expect elevated volatility rather than a clean decline.

Trading View: Near-term range expected at 285-310¢. A break above 310¢ could trigger additional short covering toward 320¢. A break below 290¢ would retest 275¢. Key monitoring points: Brazilian April rainfall, ICE robusta stocks (whether 3,977 lots is breached), real trajectory, CFTC positioning shifts, Strait of Hormuz developments, and European/US stock data.

Until Brazil's new crop begins flowing, the market is likely to move along four axes — stocks, exports, currency, and geopolitics. Both roasters and traders should balance near-term volatility management with medium-term downside risk hedging.
