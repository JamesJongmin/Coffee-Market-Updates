Bi-Weekly Market Summary (May 9–23)

Over the past two weeks, the coffee futures market was dominated by the onset of Brazil's bumper crop harvest and global surplus projections on the downside, while the surge in El Niño probability emerged as a new upside variable. July arabica (KCN26) settled at 272.35¢ on May 22, down approximately 8.7% from two weeks prior, trading in an 18-month low zone.

During the first week (May 9–16), seasonal supply pressure intensified as Brazil's new crop harvest commenced. On May 7, the Coffee Trading Academy projected Brazil's 2026/27 coffee harvest would increase by 12% y/y to 71.4 million bags, reinforcing the bearish tone. In the second week (May 16–22), NOAA's El Niño warning and dry weather concerns in Vietnam triggered short-covering in robusta (+1.68%), though arabica remained under pressure.

Key Structural Shift: The most significant development over the past two weeks is the dramatic shift in speculative positioning. CFTC data (as of May 19) shows non-commercial traders holding a net position of -78,909 contracts — a massive net short. Simultaneously, NOAA raised El Niño probability to 82% (May–Jul), with a 67% chance of a "Super El Niño," introducing weather risk for the 2026/27 crop cycle as a new upside factor.

Fundamental Analysis

Brazil's 2026/27 coffee harvest is now underway as of May, transforming the record crop forecasts into physical supply. Major agencies continue to project historically high output levels.

According to Cecafé, Brazil's April coffee exports totaled 3.122 million bags, up +0.6% y/y, showing the first signs of recovery after months of decline. However, export revenue fell -17.7% to $1.109 billion, reflecting lower international prices. Cumulative Jan–Apr exports stood at 11.62 million bags, down -16.1% y/y. Cecafé expects canephora (conilon/robusta) shipments to recover from May onward, with arabica following in July as the new harvest progresses.

Notably, robusta/conilon exports surged +374% y/y in April, indicating that new crop canephora volumes are entering the market first, while arabica's share of exports declined to 77.3%.

2. El Niño: A New Market Variable

NOAA El Niño Alert (May Update): El Niño emergence probability at 82% for May–Jul, with 96% chance of persistence through Dec–Feb 2026/27. "Super El Niño" (strong or very strong) probability at 67%. The Niño-3.4 region temperature anomaly is forecast to reach approximately +1.8°C by Q4 2026, exceeding the "strong" classification threshold of +1.5°C.

El Niño's impact on the coffee market is multifaceted. Coffee trader Commercial warned that the El Niño pattern may delay rains in Brazil during September and October, when tree flowering normally occurs, potentially damaging the 2026/27 crop cycle (effectively the 2027/28 harvest). However, the impact on the current bumper crop being harvested is limited. The wet anomaly forecast for May–August in Brazilian arabica regions affects quality rather than quantity, according to some analysts.

The impact on Vietnam and Indonesia is more direct. Vaisala reported that recent showers in Vietnam's Central Highlands have been spotty, and more rain is needed to aid cherry growth. Rabobank's Carlos Mera noted that El Niño forecasts are stoking concerns about potential dryness in Vietnam and parts of Indonesia.

3. Vietnam: Surging Exports Meet Dry Weather Concerns

Vietnam, the world's largest robusta producer, is sending conflicting signals. According to Vietnam's National Statistics Office, 2026 coffee exports (Jan–Apr) rose +15.8% y/y to 810,000 MT. Annual 2025 exports surged +17.5% to 1.58 MMT, and 2025/26 production is projected to climb +6% to a 4-year high of 1.76 MMT (29.4 million bags).

However, dry weather conditions in the Central Highlands emerged in May, serving as the primary catalyst for robusta's +1.68% rally on May 22. While surging export volumes remain bearish, El Niño-driven dry weather forecasts add upside risk to the 2026/27 crop outlook.

ICE certified inventories remain at low levels for both varieties, supporting the price floor. Arabica certified stocks stood at 449,567 bags on May 22 — a 3-month low — continuing a two-month downtrend. Robusta inventories fell to a 2-year low of 3,631 lots last Friday (May 16) before recovering to 3,968 lots (6-week high) by May 22.

Absolute inventory levels remain historically depressed, suggesting that physical market tightness persists despite the Brazilian bumper crop. It will take several more weeks before new crop volumes begin flowing into certified stocks in meaningful quantities.

CFTC data (as of May 19) shows non-commercial traders holding a net short position of -78,909 contracts. Open interest stands at 970,925 contracts. While longs increased by 12,829 contracts and shorts decreased by 5,680 week-over-week, the net short bias remains overwhelming.

Open interest rose by 9,502 contracts during the reporting period (May 12–19). This massive speculative net short position reflects the market's aggressive pricing of the surplus narrative, but simultaneously represents substantial short-covering potential should unexpected supply disruptions — such as El Niño-driven crop damage — materialize.

6. Strait of Hormuz Closure & Logistics

The effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz, which began in late February, continues into May. Commercial vessel throughput stands at approximately 5% of pre-war averages, with over 1,550 vessels and 22,500 seafarers stranded. Shipping lines are rerouting via the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10–14 days to Asia–Europe voyages, with emergency surcharges reaching up to $3,000/FEU.

The impact on the coffee market is tangible. Rising shipping rates, insurance premiums, fuel costs, and fertilizer prices are increasing costs for coffee importers and roasters. Shipments of arabica from East Africa and Vietnam to European destinations face particular delays, tightening near-term physical supply.

7. Other Key Developments

Brazil Q1 Export Slump: According to Cecafé, Brazil's Q1 2026 coffee exports totaled 8.4 million bags, down -21.2% y/y. Export revenue fell -13.6% to $3.371 billion. Contributing factors included producers holding back ahead of the new harvest, port logistics challenges, and the deteriorating international trade environment.

US Tariff Impact: Following the imposition of 50% tariffs on Brazilian coffee, US-bound exports plunged -41.5% in Jan–Apr. American roasters continue to hold off on major contracts with Brazil, awaiting progress in trade negotiations. Exports to Germany also fell -12.9%, while shipments to Italy (+3.2%) and Belgium (+15.4%) increased.

USDA FAS Outlook: World coffee production in 2025/26 is projected to rise +2.0% y/y to a record 178.85 million bags — arabica down -4.7% (95.52M bags), robusta up +10.9% (83.33M bags). Ending stocks forecast at 20.15 million bags, down -5.4% y/y.

Market Outlook & Implications

Near-Term Market Structure

The market is caught between medium-term surplus expectations (Brazilian bumper crop + StoneX's 10M-bag global surplus) and near-term inventory tightness (ICE certified stocks at historic lows). The addition of El Niño as a new variable has deepened the directional disconnect between short- and medium-term outlooks.

The futures curve maintains a clear backwardation structure. July 272.35¢ versus March '27 at 254.75¢ represents a spread of approximately 17.60¢, reflecting both near-term physical tightness and medium-term supply improvement expectations.

Technical View: Arabica remains in a Strong Sell signal on daily charts. The 267–270¢ zone was tested as an 18-month low on May 19–20 before bouncing. A break below 265¢ opens downside risk toward 220¢. Resistance stands at 285–290¢. The massive CFTC net short (-78,909 contracts) provides significant short-covering rally potential if triggered.

El Niño Market Implications

El Niño's impact on coffee operates with a lag. There is typically a 6–12 month gap between the peak of the climate event and the peak of the production impact. With the event expected to peak in late 2026, the supply tightening effect would likely manifest in H2 2027 through 2028.

The impact varies by variety. Vietnam and Indonesia — key robusta origins — face direct exposure to El Niño-driven dry anomalies, creating production downside risk. For arabica, the current Brazilian bumper crop is unaffected, but a delay in September–October rains could damage the flowering stage and negatively impact the next crop cycle (2027/28).

Risk Factors

  • Upside Risks: El Niño intensification (67% Super El Niño probability), Vietnam/Indonesia dry weather materialization, rapid CFTC short-covering, further ICE inventory declines, prolonged Hormuz crisis.
  • Downside Risks: Brazil bumper harvest ramp-up (Jul onward), 10M-bag global surplus realization, US tariff-driven demand destruction, El Niño weakening and weather premium evaporation, global economic slowdown dampening coffee consumption.

Conclusion

The coffee market is at an inflection point where "surplus realization" and "climate uncertainty" intersect. Brazil's record harvest is underway, driving prices to 18-month lows, yet the rapid emergence of El Niño has introduced meaningful uncertainty for the next crop cycle.

Near-term trading is expected within a 265–285¢ range. The floor is supported by historically low ICE inventories and roaster buying interest, while the ceiling is capped by bumper crop supply pressure and the massive speculative net short. The CFTC net short of -78,909 contracts suggests the market has largely priced in the downside, but weather events such as El Niño could trigger sharp reversals.

Key Monitoring Points: NOAA June El Niño model update (intensity confirmation), Brazil harvest progress and quality reports, ICE certified stock changes (new crop inflow timing), Vietnam Central Highlands rainfall, Strait of Hormuz negotiations, CFTC positioning shifts, US–Brazil tariff negotiations.

While the supply improvement narrative dominates the outlook for H2 2026, the combination of depleted global inventories from three consecutive deficit years (2021/22–2023/24) and El Niño risk suggests price downside rigidity at current levels. Managing risk across both directional scenarios remains essential.
