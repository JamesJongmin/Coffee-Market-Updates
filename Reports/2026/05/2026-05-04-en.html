Weekly Summary

The coffee futures market this week was caught between two opposing forces: the re-escalation of the Strait of Hormuz crisis and the strengthening outlook for a record Brazilian crop. Jul arabica (KCN26) settled at 285.50¢ on May 4, edging down 0.31% on the day. A mid-week rally driven by Hormuz tensions was capped by dollar strength and persistent expectations of Brazilian supply abundance.

The dominant market dynamic was the tug-of-war between geopolitical risk premium and medium-term surplus expectations. The US launched "Operation Project Freedom" on May 4, deploying over 100 aircraft and 15,000 service members to escort vessels through the Strait of Hormuz. However, Iran labeled the operation a ceasefire violation and resumed attacks, with a South Korean-operated vessel hit by an explosion and a fire breaking out at the Fujairah Oil Industry Zone in the UAE. Crude oil surged above $100/bbl, with attendant increases in shipping rates, insurance premiums, and fertilizer costs structurally pressuring coffee supply costs.

Key Risk: The Strait of Hormuz blockade has persisted for over 8 weeks. Baker Hughes assumes the strait may not fully reopen until H2 2026, with a Dallas Fed survey showing ~80% of energy executives expect reopening no earlier than August. The ICO has warned that rising fertilizer prices linked to the conflict could increase coffee production costs globally, potentially adding 10–15% to input costs for the 2026/27 cycle.

Fundamental Analysis

1. Hormuz Crisis & Coffee Market Transmission

Since the US-Israeli airstrikes on Iran on February 28, the Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed to commercial traffic. The impact on coffee markets operates through direct (logistics) and indirect (fertilizer/energy) channels.

Logistics channel: Elevated shipping rates and war-risk insurance premiums are directly impacting African-origin coffee exports routed via the Suez Canal (Ethiopia, Uganda, Kenya) and Asian-origin coffee transiting through the Middle East (India, Indonesia). Alternative routing adds transit time and cost, pushing up CIF prices for importers.

Fertilizer/energy channel: Disruption to LNG and natural gas flows through Hormuz is driving up global urea and fertilizer prices. This raises input costs for major producing countries including Brazil, Colombia, and Vietnam, with structural implications for 2026/27 and beyond production economics.

2. Brazil: Record Crop Outlook Reaffirmed

Brazil's 2026/27 bumper crop outlook was reinforced this week. On April 29, the Coffee Trading Academy (CTA) published a survey of 758 Brazilian farmers projecting an 11.5% y/y increase in production, translating to a total estimate of 71.4 million bags — an all-time record.

The CTA survey estimated arabica production at 47.9 million bags (+13.5% y/y) and robusta at 23.5 million bags (+7.6% y/y). Coffee acreage expanded approximately 3% y/y, with over 60% of respondents citing off-season rainfall as having a positive impact on crop development — a result of the biennial on-year cycle combined with favorable weather conditions.

However, StoneX notes that weather variability has kept yields below their theoretical maximum, and frost risk during May–July in Southern Minas Gerais, Cerrado, and São Paulo remains a critical watch factor. While the probability of severe frost is assessed as low given proximity to solar maximum, any abnormal cold front from Argentina could trigger sharp speculative rallies.

3. Vietnam: Export Surge Continues, Robusta Under Pressure

Vietnam, the world's largest robusta producer, continues to expand exports. Vietnam's National Statistics Office reported that 2026 coffee exports (Jan–Apr) rose +15.8% y/y to 810,000 MT. Full-year 2025 exports were up +17.5% y/y at 1.58 MMT.

Q1 2026 exports totaled 577,300 tons (worth approximately $2.71 billion), with volume up +12.6% y/y. However, the average export unit price fell 16.9%, resulting in a 6.4% decline in total export value — reflecting the global supply improvement dynamic weighing on prices. Germany, Italy, and Spain remain the top destination markets, while China has emerged as a notably active buyer.

ICE arabica certified stocks fell to 494,508 bags as of April 21, marking a 2.25-month low. With the May contract entering its delivery period, near-term demand for certified inventories is expected to persist. Sucafina notes that while roaster futures exposure has been substantially reduced following the April selloff, the physical market is likely to remain tight until Brazilian new-crop flows begin in Q3.

ICE robusta inventories dropped to 3,755 lots as of April 22 — a 16.25-month low — underscoring severe physical robusta scarcity. On April 15, stocks had reached 3,891 lots, the lowest level in approximately 15 months.

CFTC data (as of April 28) shows non-commercial net long positions expanded to +25,026 contracts, up 5,739 from the prior week (+19,287 as of April 21). The expansion was two-sided: long positions increased by 2,208 contracts while short positions decreased by 3,531. Open interest rose to 171,239 contracts (+8,369 w/w).

The net long expansion reflects fresh buying in response to Hormuz risk premium alongside short-covering driven by Brazil crop uncertainty. Total traders stood at 335, indicating healthy market participation.

6. Brazil Export Trends

Subdued Brazilian physical exports continue to support the price floor. Cecafe reported March green coffee exports fell -10% y/y to 2.65 million bags. Brazil's Trade Ministry data showed March coffee exports plunging -31% y/y to 151,000 MT. February green coffee exports were also down -27% y/y at 2.3 million bags.

Farmer Holdout Dynamics: In a backwardated market where futures trade below spot, Brazilian farmers are withholding supply. According to Safras & Mercado, forward sales of the 2026/27 crop stand at just 8% of production potential — well below the prior year's 9% and the historical average of 17%. Despite record crop expectations, the physical flow of coffee remains constrained by grower decisions, creating a market where spot premiums persist above fundamental fair value.

7. Dollar Strength

The dollar index firmed to $98.36 on May 4, adding downside pressure to dollar-denominated arabica prices. Simultaneously, the Brazilian real remained near a 2-year high against the dollar, reducing export incentives for Brazilian producers — a cross-currency dynamic that is suppressing both futures prices (via strong dollar) and physical supply flows (via strong real).

Market Outlook & Risk Assessment

Near-Term Market Structure

The market currently balances on three competing forces. First, the Hormuz blockade and ICE inventory lows create near-term upside pressure. Second, Brazil's record crop outlook and projected 10-million-bag global surplus generate medium-term downside pressure. Third, the cross-currents of dollar strength and real strength produce offsetting currency effects.

The cash-futures spread (Cash: 323.01¢ vs Jul: 285.50¢ = +37.51¢) indicates a steep backwardation, reflecting persistent near-term physical tightness. However, the deferred curve flattens considerably (Dec 268.60¢, Mar '27 266.05¢), suggesting the market is pricing in gradual supply normalization from Q4 onward.

Sucafina Market View: Roaster hedge coverage has improved versus last year, limiting the risk of panic buying. However, the May delivery period may sustain demand for ICE certified stocks, keeping the physical market tight until Brazilian new-crop arrivals in July. Brazil, Central America, and Colombia are all between crop cycles, creating a simultaneous supply gap.

Medium-Term Outlook

The H2 2026 trajectory hinges on the timing of physical new-crop arrivals from Brazil. The 2026/27 harvest is underway for robusta (conilon) since March–April, with arabica intensifying from May–June. Multiple forecasters converge on a 66.2–75.9 million bag range, with Rabobank's projection of an 8.64 million bag global surplus underpinning the medium-term bearish case.

However, a prolonged Hormuz blockade could reshape the cost structure entirely: rising fertilizer prices would increase 2027/28 production costs, potentially raising the medium-term price floor. Baker Hughes assumes the strait may not fully reopen until H2 2026, while a Dallas Fed survey shows approximately 80% of energy executives expect reopening no earlier than August.

Risk Factors

  • Strait of Hormuz: Blockade in its 8th week. US launched Project Freedom on May 4 but Iran declared it a ceasefire violation and resumed attacks. Structural rise in shipping, insurance, and fertilizer costs pressuring coffee import prices. Resolution timeline is the market's greatest uncertainty.
  • Brazil Frost Risk: May–July frost window in Southern Minas Gerais, Cerrado, and São Paulo. Low probability due to solar maximum proximity, but any Argentine polar air incursion could trigger sharp speculative rallies.
  • ICE Inventory Draw: Arabica stocks at 495K bags (2.25-month low), robusta at 3,755 lots (16-month low). Further drawdown during May delivery could amplify upside pressure.
  • Brazilian Farmer Holdout: Forward sales at 8% vs. 17% historical average. Physical supply delayed despite record crop potential, sustaining spot premiums.
  • Dollar & Real: Dollar strength suppresses futures; real strength suppresses Brazilian exports. Both currencies' trajectories are critical near-term price drivers.

Conclusion

The coffee market has entered a phase where two contradictory narratives — bumper crop expectations and geopolitical supply disruption — are locked in a standoff. Brazil's record harvest outlook (71–76 million bags) is an unambiguous medium-term bearish factor, but the Hormuz blockade's impact on fertilizer and logistics costs, combined with ICE inventory lows, is preventing a sharp further decline.

CFTC net long expansion (+25,026 contracts) signals that speculative capital is beginning to price Hormuz risk, while the 37.5¢ cash-futures backwardation reflects persistent physical scarcity. Brazilian farmers' reluctance to sell forward (8% pre-sold) is delaying the materialization of the bumper crop effect until Q3 at the earliest.

Trading View: Near-term range 275–300¢. Upside breakout above 300¢ possible on Hormuz re-escalation; downside risk to 275¢ and below toward 250¢ if Hormuz eases and Brazilian harvest accelerates. Key monitors: Hormuz reopening timeline, Brazil May–July frost events, ICE certified stock movements, CFTC positioning trends, USD/BRL dynamics.

Implications for the Industry

Import Cost Structure: Elevated shipping and insurance costs from the Hormuz crisis are directly impacting CIF prices for African and Asian-origin coffees. Specialty roasters relying on Ethiopian or Kenyan beans should assess alternative sourcing or increase Latin American allocations.

Hedging Strategy: The backwardated curve (cash 323¢ vs. futures 285¢) presents favorable entry points for long-dated forward purchases. However, a phased buying strategy is advisable given potential further price adjustment when Brazilian new-crop supply materializes in Q3.

Fertilizer Cost Pass-Through: If the Hormuz crisis persists, rising fertilizer input costs at origin could structurally pass through to green coffee prices, effectively raising the medium-to-long-term price floor beyond current fundamental models.
