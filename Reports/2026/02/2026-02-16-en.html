Market Overview

May arabica futures (KCK26) settled at 298.30¢ on February 13, up 0.85¢ (+0.29%) from the previous week. Prices rallied to 305.85¢ mid-week before retreating on Brazil's record production forecast. Roaster buying emerged near 6-month lows, providing support around 300¢.

Key Point: Brazil's Conab forecasts 2026 production at 66.2 million bags, the highest on record. Near-term support comes from low ICE inventories and Colombian production decline, while medium-term outlook shows improved supply from Brazil's bumper crop.

Brazil's Conab (National Supply Company) released its first 2026 crop survey on February 5, forecasting production at 66.2 million 60-kg bags, up 17.2% year-over-year. This would surpass the 2020 record of 63.1 million bags.

Arabica production is projected at 44.1 million bags, up 23.3% y/y, driven by the biennial cycle's on-year, expanded planted area, balanced weather conditions, and physiological recovery. Minas Gerais, the largest producing state, expects 32.4 million bags with improved rainfall during key flowering stages.

Weather conditions have improved significantly. According to Somar Meteorologia, Minas Gerais received 72.6mm rainfall in the week ending February 6, representing 113% of historical average. Cepea researchers note that humidity throughout most of January and early February should favor grain filling during this crucial phase.

Meanwhile, Brazil's Trade Ministry reported January coffee exports declined 42.4% y/y to 141,000 tons. However, 2025 total export value hit a record $16.1 billion, with average prices surging 57.2%.

Vietnam's National Statistics Office reported on February 10 that January coffee exports surged 38.3% y/y to 198,000 tons. Total 2025 export value reached a record $8.9 billion. Export volume increased 18.3% to 1.59 million tons, while average price jumped 53% to $5,670/ton.

2025/26 production is forecast at 1.76 million tons (29.4 million bags), up 6% y/y. The EU's classification of Vietnam as "low-risk" under EUDR (deforestation regulation) means only 1% of shipments face inspection, improving trade conditions.

VICOFA Chairman noted that prices in the 2025-26 season are unlikely to remain as strong as this season due to increased production, though forecasting remains difficult given climate risks, trade tensions, speculative activity, and currency volatility.

3. Colombia: Production Plunge

The Colombian Coffee Growers Federation reported January production plummeted 34% y/y to 893,000 bags. This supply reduction from the world's second-largest arabica producer provides near-term price support.

ICE arabica inventories reached a 3.25-month high of 462,000 bags on January 7, recovering modestly from the 1.75-year low of 397,000 bags on November 18. However, stocks remain about half of year-ago levels (approximately 1 million bags), indicating persistent tightness.

ICE robusta inventories increased from a 13-month low of 4,012 lots on December 10 to a 2-month high of 4,662 lots on January 26. While showing recovery, levels remain below historical averages.

According to CFTC (Commodity Futures Trading Commission) data as of January 27, non-commercial (speculative) traders' net short position contracted significantly. Long positions increased by 9,767 contracts while short positions decreased by 828 contracts, improving the net position to -167,753 contracts, a gain of 10,595 contracts from the prior week.

This indicates speculative short covering occurred near the 300¢ level. Funds covered shorts at 6-month lows, supporting the price rebound. However, the market remains in net short territory, suggesting prevailing pessimism about the medium-term outlook.

Commercial (hedger) traders' net long position decreased by 13,477 contracts to 176,753. While roasters bought at lower prices, the scale remained limited.

Market Dynamics & Outlook

Near-Term Market Structure

The market is balancing between near-term tightness and medium-term supply improvement outlook. Around 300¢, roaster buying provides support. Above 350¢, Brazil's bumper crop forecast and Vietnam export growth cap gains.

Technical Perspective: The 100-day and 200-day moving averages are acting as key support levels. A break below these could trigger momentum selling. The nearby-deferred spread maintains steep inversion (backwardation), reflecting near-term supply shortage.

Demand Side

Roaster buying activity is noteworthy. Recent price declines have sparked inventory replenishment demand around 300¢. US roasters in particular are showing active buying, benefiting from approximately 40¢ discount following tariff removal on Brazilian coffee.

However, Q1 2026 roaster demand is reportedly weaker than expected, raising concerns about demand destruction at elevated prices.

Medium-Term Outlook

USDA Foreign Agricultural Service (FAS) projected in its December 18 report that 2025/26 world coffee production will increase 2.0% y/y to 178.85 million bags. Arabica is expected to decline 4.7% to 95.52 million bags, while robusta should increase 10.9% to 83.33 million bags.

Brazil's 2026/27 crop is the key variable. According to Sucafina's Brazil crop tour, total production of 75.6 million bags is expected (49.7M arabica, 25.9M robusta). Record production is forecast in both Cerrado and Rondonia, with weather conditions nearly ideal. This supply will begin impacting the market from Q2 2026.

Rabobank forecasts a large supply surplus of 8.64 million bags in 2026/27, projecting arabica prices to decline by approximately one-third by Q4 2026. However, after three consecutive years of supply deficits totaling 14.6 million bags through 2024/25, inventory levels will remain relatively low.

Risk Factors

  • Brazil Weather: Currently favorable, but irregular weather patterns observed post-flowering. Excessive humidity could promote disease spread (especially coffee rust).
  • Indonesia Sumatra: Severely damaged by cyclone in late 2025. 2025-26 exports forecast to decline up to 15%.
  • Currency: Recent Brazilian real depreciation encourages exports, but futures trading below spot prices discourages forward sales.
  • Demand: Risk of demand destruction at high prices. Consumers may switch to robusta or reduce consumption.

Conclusion

The coffee market faces conflicting near-term and medium-term dynamics. Near-term support comes from low ICE inventories, Colombian production decline, and Indonesian supply disruption. Roaster buying establishes a floor around 300¢.

Medium-term, Brazil's record production forecast and Vietnam's export growth exert downward pressure. From Q2 2026, Brazil's new crop will intensify supply pressure.

Trading Perspective: Range-bound trading between 290-310¢ expected near-term. Roaster buying supports the lower end, while Brazil's bumper crop forecast caps the upper end. Key factors to monitor: Brazil February-March rainfall and flowering, roaster demand trends, and ICE inventory changes.

Looking toward H2 2026, supply improvement outlook dominates, though coffee markets always face potential weather-driven volatility. Prudent risk management is essential.
