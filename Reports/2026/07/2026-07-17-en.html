Weekly Market Summary

This week was dominated by an unwind of last week's spike. After an ICE margin-hike liquidity shock lifted arabica +16.19% in a single Monday session last week (the most since 2000), the market handed back much of that gain. KCU26 opened the week near ~330c, fell -1.18% Tuesday (326.10c), and dropped -4.33% Thursday (~312.65c) for a -4.7% week-to-date reversal.

Three forces drove the pullback. First, normalizing liquidity — the order book thinned by the margin shock gradually rebuilt, and last week's one-way lurches faded. Second, drier Brazil weather — forecasts pointing to dry conditions in key regions raised expectations that the rain-delayed harvest would resume, capping the upside. Third, supply confirmation — Cecafe reported Brazil's June green coffee exports jumped +14.4% y/y to 2.64M bags, evidence that volume is actually reaching the market.

Even so, the floor held firm. ICE arabica certified stocks hit a fresh 2.25-year low of 339,652 bags (346K the prior week), keeping spot tight, while robusta stocks recovered to 4,220 lots (a 3.5-month high). Above all, two forward risks came into focus this week — the US government announced a 25% tariff on Brazilian imports effective July 22, and the US Climate Prediction Center warned that El Nino could be the strongest in 75+ years.

Key dynamic of the week: if last week was a "microstructure spike," this week was its unwind (normalization). Rebuilt liquidity, drier weather and strong exports pulled prices back toward fundamentals (structural surplus). But the cushion of 2.25-yr-low stocks and two fresh upside catalysts — tariffs (7/22) and a 75-yr-strongest El Nino — kept the decline a "stair-step" rather than a crash.

Fundamental Analysis

1. Microstructure — Normalizing Liquidity Unwinds the Spike

If last week's spike was made by an ICE margin-hike liquidity drain, this week's pullback was its gradual normalization. As the thinned order book rebuilt, forced-liquidation lurches faded and prices drifted lower in a wide range off last week's 5.5-month highs. Much of this week's decline was thus a reversal of last week's froth rather than fresh bearish news.

Implication of the unwind: a microstructure spike unwinds when microstructure normalizes — this week is the textbook case. But in thin liquidity, both up and down overshoots remain common, so volatility management still outranks directional bets.

2. Weather & Harvest — Drier Turn and a Harvest Resumption

The real driver of this week's downside was Brazil's drier turn. Expectations grew that the rain-delayed harvest would resume as dry-weather forecasts spread. Per Safras & Mercado, the harvest was 52% complete as of July 1 — still behind last year's 60% — but a return to dry weather speeds volume flow, a near-term bearish force. Winter frost and flowering risks nonetheless persist.

3. Exports — Cecafe's June Surge Confirms Supply

Cecafe reported Brazil's June green coffee exports jumped +14.4% y/y to 2.64M bags. As evidence that new-crop volume is actually shipping, it lent weight to the "record surplus" narrative and triggered Thursday's slump. Vietnam's H1 exports were also firm at +7.3% (1.05 MMT), sustaining a medium-term drag on both arabica and robusta.

5. Macro & Positioning — Firm Real, Crowded Robusta Longs

USD/BRL: the real is near ~5.10, up 1.66% over the past month. But the US 25% tariff (effective 7/22) could weigh on US-bound exports and dollar inflows, a new brake on the real's appreciation. CFTC positioning: the COT as of 7/7 showed funds' robusta net-longs swelling to 44,195 lots (a 2-year high). A crowded long can amplify downside on any correction.

Implications

1. The unwind confirms the in-depth thesis: as argued last week in "The Surplus Paradox," a microstructure spike reverts toward fundamentals (surplus) once liquidity normalizes. This week's -4.7% pullback is the proof. For roasters and traders, last week's highs are confirmed as a hedge/sell re-set opportunity.

2. But not a crash — the low-stock cushion: stocks at a 2.25-yr low (339,652) support the floor, keeping the correction a "stair-step" rather than a crash. Downside pace stays limited until the surplus is confirmed in warehouse stocks.

3. Tariffs (7/22) are a new near-term catalyst: the US 25% tariff could distort US prices and trade flows depending on the green-coffee exemption. Physical buyers should review pre-effective sourcing and hedge timing and watch for the final exemption list.

4. El Nino/frost = residual upside: the CPC's "strongest in 75 years" warning raises Sep-Oct flowering (2027/28) and winter frost risk. Cheap call-option hedges against the bumper-crop consensus retain their value.

Market Outlook & Risks

Short-Term Structure (1-3 months)

The trend has turned to a post-spike unwind. Resistance sits at 330c (weekly high) and 350c (last week's high); support at 312c (Thursday close) and 300c. With normalizing liquidity, a drier turn and strong exports capping the top while low stocks, tariffs and El Nino support the bottom, expect a high-volatility retracement regime. Whether 300c support holds is the first fork.

Technical view: a break from the 5.5-month high (~350c) back to 312c. 330c resistance vs 312c/300c support. Gaps are possible around the tariff start (7/22) and frost headlines. Watch for added downside volatility if crowded robusta longs (44,195 lots) liquidate.

Medium-Term Outlook (3-12 months)

As Brazil's harvest advances and new-crop volume flows, the record-large surplus implied by USDA FAS (71.9M) and Rabobank (9.5M) is more likely to be confirmed in warehouses, favoring medium-term downside. But (1) 2.25-yr-low stocks, (2) El Nino's (strongest in 75 yrs) 2027/28 flowering risk, and (3) tariff-driven trade distortion act as buffers, making "structural downtrend plus intermittent policy/weather-driven spikes" the base case.

Key Risk Factors

  • Tariffs (eff. 7/22, two-way): the green-coffee exemption will drive US prices and trade flows.
  • Brazil weather (two-way): sustained dry accelerates harvest (down); renewed frost/rain is upside.
  • El Nino (2027/28, upside): CPC "strongest in 75 yrs" — asymmetric upside if flowering rains are disrupted.
  • Structural supply (downside): Cecafe +14.4%, Vietnam +7.3%, 9.5M surplus — the medium-term drag.
  • ICE stocks (floor cushion): 2.25-yr low (339,652) restrains a crash.
  • Positioning (downside): robusta net-longs 44,195 lots (2-yr high) — crowded-long liquidation risk.

In short, this is a stair-step retracement where near-term downside (normalization, harvest, exports) tugs against residual upside (low stocks, tariffs, El Nino).

Trading takeaways: a 300-330c retracement box near term. 330c resistance and 312c/300c support are the fork. Key monitors: (1) the 7/22 tariff start and green-coffee exemption, (2) Brazil dry weather/frost, (3) ICE stocks, (4) crowded robusta-long liquidation, (5) the Friday 7/17 settle, (6) CPC El Nino updates.

For roasters and importers, a balanced strategy makes sense: manage average cost with staggered buying on pullbacks, while hedging the upside cheaply with call options against a tariff- or El Nino-driven re-spike. The surplus is beginning to show in the warehouse (surging exports), yet stocks remain at a 2.25-year low — until this tug-of-war resolves, avoid one-way bets.
