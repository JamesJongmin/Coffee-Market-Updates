Weekly Market Summary

Coffee futures staged a V-shaped surge, reversing the prior week's weakness. September arabica (KCU26) opened the week +4.60¢ (+1.68%) Monday, jumped +6.71% Tuesday alone (the biggest daily gain since 2022), and rose another +13.45¢ (+4.54%) Wednesday to reach ~309.30¢, a 4.75-month high. On Thursday (7/2) it fell -8.70¢ (-2.81%) to ~300.60¢ on pre-holiday profit-taking and long liquidation — but the week still closed up +27.95¢ (+10.2%) versus the prior Friday's 272.65¢. Friday (7/3) was a holiday closure, so the week ran to just four sessions.

The core trigger was a rain-driven halt to Brazil's harvest. Per Somar Meteorologia, Minas Gerais — the top arabica state — received 31.3 mm of rain in the week through June 28, or 1,956% of the historical average, an extraordinary total. Heavy rain in the heart of the harvest window stopped fieldwork and raised drying/sorting quality concerns, immediately spurring buying.

The Japan Meteorological Agency's confirmation of El Niño then poured fuel on the upside. When the JMA confirmed on Wednesday that an El Niño pattern had formed across the equatorial Pacific, short covering emerged, and the U.S. NOAA put the odds of persistence at 67%. El Niño can delay Brazil's September–October flowering rains, threatening the 2026/27 crop, extending the market's focus from the immediate harvest disruption to next season's risk. ICE arabica certified stocks falling further to a 2.25-year low of 375,079 bags reaffirmed spot tightness and underpinned the advance.

Key dynamic of the week: the three upside pillars that had collapsed last week (rain → harvest delay, weather risk, low stocks) were all rebuilt in a single week — rain halted the harvest again, the JMA's El Niño confirmation revived flowering risk, and stocks set another 2.25-year low. Yet Thursday's -2.81% pre-holiday pullback was a reminder that this surge rests on weather and sentiment, while the structural surplus (StoneX 10M / Rabobank arabica 9.5M) still looms overhead. The near term is a tight standoff between weather-driven upside and structural oversupply.

Fundamental Analysis

1. Weather & Harvest — extraordinary rain halts the harvest

The biggest driver of the week's surge was a rain-driven halt to Brazil's harvest. Somar Meteorologia reported that Minas Gerais received 31.3 mm (1,956% of average) in the week through June 28. May–August is normally a dry harvest window, but the exceptional downpour stopped fieldwork and raised concerns over cherry drying and sorting quality. It was a precise reversal of last week's "dry return → harvest resumes" narrative — a reminder of how sensitive near-term prices are to Brazil weather headlines.

The dual nature of harvest disruption: heavy rain brings not only immediate logistics/fieldwork disruption (upside) but also defect and mold risk from higher humidity that can degrade quality grades (upside). But if the rain clears and dryness returns, resumption hopes could swing prices back down quickly — as they did last week — leaving weather the key wildcard for two-way volatility.

2. El Niño — flowering risk officially ignited

If the harvest disruption was the near-term trigger, the JMA's confirmation of El Niño was the structural catalyst that extended the market's gaze to next season. The JMA confirmed El Niño formation across the equatorial Pacific on Wednesday, and NOAA put persistence odds at 67%. Perfect Daily Grind noted some traders are watching what they call a "Godzilla" El Niño. The core concern is that El Niño could delay Brazil's Sept–Oct flowering rains and impair the 2026/27 (and some 27/28) crop. Combined with Brazilian farmers holding back sales on higher-price hopes, El Niño has emerged as a lingering upside risk that counters the record-surplus consensus.

ICE arabica certified stocks set another 2.25-year low of 375,079 bags Wednesday, down from 377,465 Tuesday and further below the prior week (~385K). Despite the record-crop outlook, it reaffirms that old-crop spot supply stays tight until new-crop coffee is certified — adding weight to this week's rain/El Niño upside. Robusta stocks, by contrast, rebounded from a 2-year low of 3,631 lots on May 15 to 4,053 lots (a 2.75-month high) Monday, easing robusta's spot tightness somewhat.

Even amid the weather-driven surge, the structural-oversupply outlook strengthened. Rabobank raised its 2026/27 global arabica surplus estimate to 9.5M bags from 7.0M. That aligns with StoneX's prior-week projection of record 182.5M-bag global production and a ~10M-bag surplus (a 6-year high), and it is likely to re-price into the market once new-crop volume begins converting into certified stocks. In other words, this week's upside was a weather/sentiment rebound, while the medium-term fundamental weight still sits to the downside. The timing asymmetry between old-crop tightness (low stocks) and new-crop abundance (widening surplus) remains the basis for backwardation and intermittent spikes.

5. Exports — Brazil & Vietnam supply pressure persists

Brazil's export association Cecafé reported on June 11 that May green coffee exports rose +4.2% y/y to 2.73M bags. Exports from Vietnam, the world's largest robusta producer, maintained a firm pace, a structural drag on robusta. That robusta rose more modestly than arabica this week (Wed +3.09%, Thu +0.32%) while robusta stocks rebounded to a 2.75-month high is not unrelated to Vietnamese supply pressure. New-crop inflows and export pace are medium-term downside factors for both arabica and robusta.

6. Macro & Positioning — real modestly weaker, COT delayed

USD/BRL: The real closed at ~5.22 on July 2, trading in a 5.16 (6/30 low) – 5.21 (7/2) range and ending modestly weaker versus the prior week's 5.17. A weaker real tends to encourage Brazilian export sales and is typically bearish for arabica, but this week the rain/El Niño upside overwhelmed the currency. FX was a cushion to the rally, not a primary driver.

CFTC speculative positioning: The latest published reading (as of June 23) showed managed-money net longs in Coffee C at 20,918 contracts (44,184 long / 23,266 short; open interest 186,617), up from the prior cross-checked 11,863 (as of 6/9). The June 30 COT — which would capture this week's surge — was delayed by the 7/3 holiday (expected 7/6) and is not reflected here; it will be confirmed next week (no premature estimation).

Implications

1. Weather headlines dominate — two-way, high volatility: last week's "dry → harvest resumes (downside)" flipped to this week's "downpour → harvest halts (upside)" — a full reversal in seven days. Near-term prices are governed by Brazil weather and El Niño headlines more than fundamentals, a high-volatility regime. Managing two-way volatility via options is more sensible than a directional bet; if the rain clears, a fast retracement — as last week — is a real risk.

2. El Niño confirmed — a lingering upside made real: with JMA confirmation and NOAA at 67%, El Niño has moved from assumption to fact. If Sept–Oct flowering rains are delayed, 27/28 crop concerns could resurface and unsettle the record-surplus consensus. In a market fixated on a big crop, cheap call-option-based asymmetric upside hedges arguably offer better value now.

3. The structural surplus is intact — rallies are a hedging opportunity: Rabobank's arabica surplus of 9.5M (raised) and StoneX's 10M show the medium-term downside weight has, if anything, grown heavier. Because this week's +10.2% surge is a weather-driven bounce, roasters and traders can use elevated levels to rebalance sales/hedges. Avoid chasing above 300¢.

4. Stocks at a 2.25-year low — a floor against sharp drops: stocks down to 375K (still around half of a year ago) suggest that until new-crop coffee is certified, spot tightness supports the floor. This both amplified the week's surge and argues for a "stair-step" retracement rather than a crash on any correction.

5. Roasters & importers — hedging discipline in a spike: a +36¢ move over three sessions followed by Thursday's pullback signals rising volatility. Physical buyers needing coverage should cap the upside with calls/call spreads during spikes and manage average cost via scaled buying on pullbacks, given the structural surplus.

Market Outlook & Risks

Near-term structure (1–3 months)

The trend flipped to a weather-driven near-term rally, and Wednesday's ~309¢ high reset near-term resistance to 309¢. First resistance is 309¢ (this week's high, a 4.75-month high); support runs 300¢ → 290¢ → 277¢ (Monday's close). With the downpour, harvest halt, El Niño confirmation and low stocks lifting the ceiling, and the structural surplus (Rabobank/StoneX) and a weaker real capping it, a high-volatility regime is likely. Whether 300¢ support holds is the first test; if the weather calms, a fast retracement — as last week — must be kept open.

Technical view: a +36¢ move over three sessions then Thursday's -2.81% pullback signals near-term overheating and profit-taking. 309¢ resistance vs 300¢ support is the first pivot; a break of 300¢ opens 290¢/277¢. Both ceiling and floor are sensitive to weather/El Niño headlines; expect elevated volatility. Watch backwardation and roll structure.

Medium-term outlook (3–12 months)

As Brazil's harvest resumes and progresses, new-crop inflows will accelerate. If the record-scale surplus flagged by Rabobank (arabica 9.5M, raised) and StoneX (10M surplus, 182.5M production) materializes, downside pressure should dominate into Q4 2026. But ① 2.25-year-low stocks, ② the JMA-confirmed El Niño's Sept–Oct flowering risk, and ③ whether Brazil's heavy rain persists act as cushions, making "structural decline + intermittent weather/El Niño-driven spikes" the base case rather than a trending collapse. This week's surge is a textbook example of such a spike.

Key risk factors

  • Brazil weather (two-way, key): persistent rain supports via harvest disruption/quality loss; a dry return and resuming harvest is a fast downside — as last week. The highest-volatility variable, flipping within a single week.
  • El Niño / flowering (Sept–Oct, upside): JMA confirmed, NOAA 67%. Delayed flowering rain would make 27/28 crop concerns an asymmetric upside risk — a lingering upside made real.
  • Structural supply (downside): Rabobank arabica surplus 9.5M (raised), StoneX 10M, record Brazil crop, rising Vietnam exports — the core medium-term bearish driver persists.
  • ICE stocks (floor cushion): 2.25-year low (375K). Spot tightness restrains sharp drops until new-crop conversion — and amplifies spikes.
  • FX (downside): USD/BRL ~5.22, a modestly weaker real. Further weakness would encourage Brazilian sales, a downside factor.
  • Speculative positioning (two-way): managed-money net long 20,918 (6/23), expanded. The 6/30 COT (capturing the surge) was delayed by the 7/3 holiday (expected 7/6) — confirm next week.

In short, near-term upside (weather, El Niño, stocks) squarely confronts medium-term downside (a record-scale surplus).

Trading takeaway: a near-term 277–309¢ high-volatility range. 300¢ support and 309¢ resistance are the first pivot; a break of 300¢ opens 290¢/277¢. Key monitors: ① Brazil rain persistence / harvest resumption ② El Niño flowering signals (JMA/NOAA) ③ ICE stock trend ④ the 6/30 COT (released 7/6, capturing the surge) ⑤ USD/BRL 5.15–5.25 ⑥ surplus-estimate updates from Rabobank, StoneX and others.
