Weekly Market Summary

The week's story was "the unwind completes, then a rebound." The arabica surge driven by ICE margin hikes gave back the remainder of its gains into late last week, leaving September at ~314¢ on Friday (7/17), down ~4.2% — its first lower weekly close in six weeks. But the unwind largely ended there. Friday itself firmed +1.45¢ (+145 pts), confirming support near 315¢, and the new week opened firmer (Mon +1.33%, Tue +0.66%), recovering ~2.5% to ~320¢.

The driver of the rebound was Brazil's delayed harvest. The 2026/27 harvest was 64% complete as of July 15, behind last year's 77% and the five-year average of 70%. Fresh rain forecasts — following last week's downpours — revived fears of harvest slippage and quality loss, supporting the near-term bid. Minas Gerais received just 0.2mm of rain (20% of normal) in the week to July 19, but the prospect of returning rain kept prices supported.

The floor was again underpinned by low inventories. ICE certified arabica stocks fell to 344,269 bags on Friday, a weekly drop of 28,749 bags (the largest in months), and are down 487,450 bags (-55%+) from 831,719 a year ago. That said, roughly 105,000 bags are pending grading, leaving room for stocks to rebuild as new-crop coffee is certified.

Key dynamic of the week: If the prior week was "the spike" and the weekend "the unwind," this week was a harvest-delay rebound. With near-term upside (harvest delay, low stocks, a firm real) tugging against medium-term downside (a record crop, surplus, strong exports), the US 25% tariff effective today (7/22) emerges as a new swing variable.

Fundamental Analysis

1. Production Outlook — A Record Brazil Crop and the Shift to Surplus

What defines medium-term prices is a record-scale Brazil crop. USDA FAS forecasts Brazil's 2026/27 output at a record 71.9 million bags (+14% y/y). StoneX sees Brazil arabica at 47.2 million bags (+29.3%) and a global surplus of ~10 million bags (a sharp reversal from 1.8M in 2025). Rabobank likewise projects a global surplus of 7-10 million bags and raised its arabica surplus estimate to 9.5 million bags (from 7.0M), with Brazil near 73M. Analyst arabica price targets cluster at $2.50-3.00.

Implication: The 2021-2024 deficit cycle is ending and a structural surplus cycle is opening. But the surplus is still a "forecast" — until it shows up as warehouse stocks, low inventories, weather and policy can keep triggering relief rallies.

2. Weather & Harvest — Delay Supports the Near-Term Bid

The real driver of this week's rebound was the harvest delay. Brazil's harvest was 64% complete on July 15, behind last year (77%) and the five-year average (70%). Fresh rain forecasts, following last week's downpours, revived fears of slippage and quality loss, supporting near-term prices. Beyond that, the US Climate Prediction Center's warning of an El Niño potentially the strongest in 75+ years keeps an asymmetric upside risk alive around the Sep-Oct flowering window (2027/28 crop) and winter frost.

3. Exports — Brazil and Vietnam Volumes Anchor the Medium-Term Downside

Supply-side volume keeps flowing. Vietnam's H1 (Jan-Jun) exports were robust at ~1.1 million MT (+~10% y/y), and Brazil new-crop shipments are underway. Yet, as Colombia's differential surge (May +454% y/y) shows, origin liquidity for washed arabica is deteriorating, sustaining a two-tier structure of "ample volume but tight premium-coffee availability."

5. Macro & Positioning — A Firm Real, Crowded Robusta Longs

USD/BRL: The real firmed to 5.07 on 7/21 (-0.76%), holding a one-month strengthening trend (weekly range 5.06-5.14). The real's resilience despite the US 25% tariff (effective 7/22) is supportive for coffee (it weakens the incentive for Brazilian selling). CFTC positioning (as of 7/14): Coffee C managed money was net long ~32,834 contracts (43,208 long / 10,374 short); arabica net length rose to 25,511 (gross short cut 4,625). Notably, London robusta fund longs exceed 42% of total open interest — a crowding that could amplify downside volatility as positions roll or unwind into September delivery over the next 3-4 weeks.

Implications

1. The unwind is done; it's now "supply vs. policy": The microstructure-driven spike has largely reversed, and prices are again responding to fundamentals (harvest, stocks) and policy (tariffs). For roasters and traders, ~314¢ marked the unwind's floor, and the ~320¢ rebound shows the force of the harvest-delay catalyst.

2. The tariff (today, 7/22) is the top near-term catalyst: The US 25% tariff could sharply distort US prices, differentials and sourcing depending on whether green coffee is exempted. US end-users should immediately check the exemption list and premiums for alternative origins (Colombia, Central America).

3. The surplus is a forecast; low stocks are reality: The record crop and surplus (10M) are a "forecast," but stocks at a 2-year low (344K) are "reality." Until that gap narrows via warehouse restocking, the pace of decline stays limited and relief rallies can recur.

4. El Niño / frost = residual asymmetric upside: The CPC's "strongest in 75 years" warning raises Sep-Oct flowering (2027/28) and winter-frost risk. Low-cost call-option hedges against the record-crop/surplus consensus retain their value.

Market Outlook & Risks

Short-Term Structure (1-3 months)

The trend has turned to a rebound off the unwind's floor. Resistance sits at 330¢ (prior intraweek high) and 350¢ (the spike high); support at 314¢ (weekly close) and 300¢. With harvest delay, low stocks and a firm real underpinning the floor while a record crop, surplus and tariff uncertainty cap the top, expect a high-volatility range. The green-coffee exemption question around today's tariff (7/22) is the first watershed.

Technical view: Rebounded from a ~314¢ weekly close to ~320¢. 330¢ resistance vs 314/300¢ support. Gaps possible on tariff and frost headlines. Watch for added downside volatility if crowded robusta longs (42%+ of OI) unwind on the September roll.

Medium-Term Outlook (3-12 months)

As Brazil's harvest advances and new-crop supply flows, the record-scale surplus projected by USDA FAS (71.9M), StoneX (10M surplus) and Rabobank (9.5M arabica surplus) is likely to be confirmed in warehouses, favoring medium-term downside. However, (1) 2-year-low inventories, (2) El Niño's (strongest in 75 years) 2027/28 flowering risk, and (3) tariff-driven trade distortion act as cushions/disruptors, making "structural downtrend plus intermittent policy/weather spikes" the base case. Analyst arabica targets cluster at $2.50-3.00.

Key Risk Factors

  • Tariff (effective today 7/22, two-way): The green-coffee exemption decision drives US prices, differentials and trade flows.
  • Brazil weather (two-way): Returning rain delays harvest/hurts quality (up), while sustained dryness accelerates harvest (down).
  • El Niño (2027/28, upside): CPC's "strongest in 75 years" — disrupted flowering-season rain is an asymmetric upside.
  • Structural supply (downside): Brazil 71.9M, surplus 10M, Vietnam +10% keep the medium-term bias soft.
  • ICE stocks (floor cushion): 2-year low (344K, weekly -28,749) limits sharp drops; but ~105K pending grading is a rebuild catalyst.
  • Positioning (downside): Robusta fund longs at 42%+ of OI — September roll/unwind risk.

Comprehensive Analysis

This week crossed the completion of last week's unwind with a harvest-delay rebound. September arabica settled ~314¢ on Friday (7/17), down 4.2% on the week (its first drop in six), then recovered ~2.5% to ~320¢ on 7/20-21 on Brazil's harvest delay (64% done by 7/15). Near-term upside (harvest delay, low stocks, a firm real) is in tight balance with medium-term downside (a record crop, surplus, strong exports).

On top of that, the US 25% tariff on Brazil, effective today (7/22), stands as the biggest swing variable. As long as the forecast-vs-reality gap — surplus still a projection, stocks still at a 2-year low — persists, downside is more likely to come as recurring relief-rally pullbacks than an outright collapse.

Trading takeaways: A near-term 300-330¢ range, with 314/300¢ support and 330¢ resistance as the watershed. Key monitors: (1) 7/22 tariff and green-coffee exemption; (2) Brazil rain and harvest pace; (3) ICE stocks and pending grading; (4) robusta crowded-long September roll; (5) the new COT (as of 7/21); (6) El Niño CPC updates.

For roasters and importers, a reasonable approach is to manage average cost via scaled buying near the unwind's floor while hedging the top with low-cost call options against a tariff- or El Niño-driven re-spike. Until the structural surplus is confirmed in warehouse stocks, one-way bets warrant caution.
