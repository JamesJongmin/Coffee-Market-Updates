Structural High Price Era Confirmed

December 7, 2025 Analysis Period: Nov 30 - Dec 6, 2025 Focus: KCH26 (March 2026 Contract)

Coffee Holds Firm Post-Tariff: Structural Shift to Higher Pricing

Despite President Trump's full removal of the 40% tariff on Brazilian coffee (announced November 21), coffee futures have demonstrated remarkable price resilience, rebounding to 403¢ on December 2 after an initial drop to 385¢. As of December 5, March 2026 arabica futures closed at 374.85¢, still up 36.83% year-over-year and maintaining the 300-400¢ "new normal" range.

This price action confirms a fundamental thesis: the coffee market has undergone a structural shift to elevated pricing driven by supply tightness, not temporary policy distortions. ICE arabica inventories at a 24-year low of 406,000 bags, Brazil's inventory forecasted to hit a 25-year low of 1.2 million bags, and Volcafe's projection of an 8.5 million bag arabica deficit for the 5th consecutive year all underscore this structural reality.

The tariff removal served as a critical market test. The fact that prices dropped only to 385¢ (still 40% above 2024 levels) and quickly rebounded demonstrates that fundamentals—not policy—now drive pricing. Open Interest at 80,797 contracts indicates sustained market participation, while the 71% La Niña probability from NOAA threatens the 2026/27 Brazilian crop.

Past Week Headlines (Nov 30 - Dec 6)

The past week witnessed extreme volatility as the market navigated between tariff removal relief and persistent supply tightness. Prices surged from the November 21 low of 385¢ to a December 2 high of 403¢, before moderating to current levels around 374¢. The key question: "Why do prices remain elevated despite tariff removal?"

Nov 21 (Thu)

Trump Removes 40% Tariff on Brazilian Coffee

President Trump fully eliminated the 40% tariff on Brazilian green coffee with retroactive application to shipments arriving after November 13. Instant coffee remains excluded, with ongoing negotiations. Arabica futures immediately dropped 5% to 385¢, the lowest since mid-October.

Market Impact: Short-term Bearish (-5%)
Nov 24 (Sun)

Brazil Rainfall Forecast Creates Bearish Pressure

Climatempo forecasted heavy rainfall to continue throughout the week in Brazil's coffee-growing regions, providing favorable conditions for next season's crop development. Market feared additional downside from combined tariff removal and weather improvement, though actual price decline remained limited.

Market Impact: Bearish Sentiment
Dec 1 (Sun)

Vietnam Domestic Prices Drop 6,000 Dong Monthly

Vietnam Central Highlands coffee prices fell to 111,300-112,500 dong/kg, down approximately 6,000 dong over November, signaling harvest acceleration. Lam Dong at 111,300, Dak Lak at 112,300, Dak Nong at 112,500. However, robusta futures January contract rose $26 to $4,565/ton, indicating local price drops didn't translate to global weakness.

Market Impact: Mixed
Dec 1 (Sun)

Volcafe Projects 5th Consecutive Year of Arabica Deficit

Global coffee trader Volcafe forecasted an 8.5 million bag arabica supply deficit for 2025/26, marking the 5th consecutive year of shortage. Revised Brazil arabica production down 11 million bags year-over-year to 34.4 million bags. Analysis states "climate change prevents Brazil from recovering 'on-cycle' production above 50 million bags." ICE inventories expected to reach "untenable levels" by Q2 2025.

Market Impact: Structural Bull Confirmed
Dec 2 (Mon)

Arabica Rebounds to 403¢, Tariff Impact Limited

Just 9 trading days after the November 21 low of 385¢, futures rebounded to 403.65¢ (+4.8%). December contract at 403.65¢, March at 376.65¢. Brazilian real strength and continued ICE inventory decline supported prices. Market participants noted "tariff removal created less downward pressure than anticipated."

Market Impact: Bullish Reversal (+4.8%)
Dec 4 (Wed)

Futures Break 410¢, Approaching 9-Month High

Arabica coffee CFD surged to 410.65¢ (+1.71%), approaching the 9-month high of 423¢ set on November 11. Despite partial tariff relief, Brazil's poor harvest, Vietnam flooding, and ICE inventories at lowest since March 2024 supported prices. Speculative funds and market uncertainty amplified volatility.

Market Impact: Sharp Rally
Dec 5 (Thu)

USDA Vietnam Production Forecast at 30.8M Bags (-0.6%)

USDA released Vietnam 2025/26 coffee production at 30.8 million bags, down 200,000 bags (-0.6%) from June forecast of 31 million. Composition: 29.6M robusta, 1.2M arabica. Up 6.2% year-over-year but below market expectations. Domestic consumption rising to 4.9M bags and exporter inventory declining (93.9M→83.9M bags) constrains actual export capacity.

Market Impact: Neutral
Dec 5 (Thu)

Brazil Robusta Production Hits Record 20.7M Bags

Brazil's Conab estimated 2025/26 robusta production at 20.7 million bags, with USDA forecasting 24.1 million bags—more than double 2016/17 levels. Robusta's drought tolerance and disease resistance highlighted. However, total coffee production at 56.5 million bags reflects 9.7% arabica decline. Robusta futures plunged 19.1% since early December ($5,694→$4,604).

Market Impact: Mixed
Dec 5 (Thu)

Current Price 374.85¢, Correction Phase

ICE arabica March contract closed at 374.85¢ (-1.48%, -5.65¢), down approximately 7% from December 2 high of 403¢. Intraday high 378.40¢, low 373.25¢. Previous close 380.50¢. Open Interest maintained at 80,797 contracts, indicating sustained market participation. Still up 36.83% year-over-year.

Market Impact: Short-term Correction (-1.48%)
Weekly Market Summary
The past week's action demonstrates the market successfully "passing the tariff removal test." Despite the significant November 21 removal of the 40% tariff, prices only declined to 385¢ before immediately rebounding to record 403¢ on December 2, confirming structural market strength. While Vietnamese domestic price drops, improved Brazilian rainfall, and downward USDA production revisions created short-term crosscurrents, Volcafe's 5th consecutive year of 8.5 million bag deficit projection and ICE inventories at 24-year lows support medium-to-long-term elevated pricing. Current levels around 374¢ represent a "healthy correction within a structural bull market."

Structural Supply Deficit: ICE Inventory at Historic Low

ICE arabica certified inventory stands at 406,000 bags as of December 1, maintaining 24-year low levels. After touching 398,000 bags on November 20, inventories have recovered marginally but remain at historically extreme lows. Brazilian inventories are forecasted to reach 1.2 million bags by mid-2025, a 25-year low.

More concerning is Brazil's weather situation. Conab has already revised its 2025 arabica production down 4.9% to 35.2 million bags, with the 2025/26 season projected to decline 12.4% to 34.7 million bags due to drought and high temperatures. The critical flowering phase has been exposed to dry and hot conditions, causing severe damage.

NOAA forecasts a 71% probability of La Niña conditions from October to December, which could bring additional dry weather to South America. Even the 2026/27 season faces threats. Volcafe expects a global arabica deficit of 8.5 million bags for the 2025/26 season, marking the 5th consecutive year of supply shortage.

Supply Crisis Deepens
The global coffee market faces a structural supply crisis that cannot be resolved by short-term weather improvements or policy changes. ICE and Brazilian inventories at multi-decade lows, combined with 5 consecutive years of deficits, have eliminated buffer capacity. Any weather shock—particularly La Niña-induced drought in 2026/27—could trigger price spikes. Current elevated pricing reflects this fundamental reality, not speculation.

In-Depth USDA Vietnam Report Analysis

USDA released Vietnam's 2025/26 season production at 30.8 million bags on December 5. This represents a 200,000 bag (-0.6%) downward revision from June's official forecast of 31 million bags, tempering market optimism. The breakdown shows 29.6 million bags of robusta and 1.2 million bags of arabica, up 6.2% from the 2024/25 season's 29 million bags.

Several critical factors drove USDA's downward revision. First, recent flooding and heavy rainfall have delayed harvest. As of mid-December, Central Highlands harvest progress stands at approximately 36%, slower than the historical average. Second, despite expanded acreage, per-unit productivity gains remain limited. Third, rapidly growing domestic consumption (2025/26 forecast at 4.9 million bags) reduces export availability below initial expectations.

Export projections deserve particular attention. USDA forecasts total Vietnamese exports at 27 million bags for 2025/26, with green beans accounting for 23.7 million bags and instant/roasted coffee at 3.3 million bags. While green beans constitute approximately 88% of total exports, this percentage has gradually declined from 91.2% in 2022. Vietnam is transitioning from simple raw material exports toward value-added products.

Ending stocks are projected at 839,000 bags, down 100,000 bags from the prior year's 939,000 bags. High prices and cash flow constraints have led exporters to reduce inventory holdings. This makes Vietnam more vulnerable to supply shocks, raising the possibility of sharp export declines in the event of weather disruptions.

Regarding pricing, USDA estimated the 2025/26 average export price at $5,630 per ton, representing a 143% year-over-year increase. Domestic robusta prices also doubled to $4.82 per kilogram compared to 2024 levels. This indicates Vietnamese producers operate in an extremely favorable pricing environment, with high likelihood of supply reductions in response to any future price declines.

USDA Analysis Implications
The core message from USDA's report is that Vietnam's production increase falls short of market expectations. The downward revision from June, inventory decline, and domestic consumption growth all point to constrained export capacity. More importantly, no matter how much Vietnam increases production, it's primarily robusta—which cannot substitute for declining Brazilian arabica due to structural limitations. USDA data clearly demonstrates why the simplistic equation "Vietnam production increase = price decline" doesn't function in reality.

Market Outlook: Structural High Pricing with Continued Volatility

Synthesizing the analysis above, the coffee market has clearly entered a "structural level-up" phase. The fact that prices only declined to 385¢ following the significant November 21 tariff removal, then rebounded to 403¢ within two weeks, provides decisive evidence of how tight market fundamentals have become. The current 374.85¢ level remains 36.83% above year-ago levels.

6-12 Month Price Outlook: The central scenario targets a 350-420¢ range. Downside support comes from ICE inventory at a 24-year low (406K bags), Brazilian inventory forecasted at a 25-year low (1.2M bags), and Volcafe's projection of a 5th consecutive year of 8.5 million bag arabica deficit. A drop below 300¢ appears highly unlikely, with strong buying interest expected to emerge at 320-330¢ even in the event of short-term corrections.

Upside Risks: The biggest variable is Brazilian weather for the 2026/27 season. If NOAA's 71% La Niña forecast materializes, South American dry weather could intensify, potentially causing additional production declines in already-impacted Brazil. In this scenario, prices could rise to 450-500¢, with the February high of 441¢ potentially being challenged in extreme cases.

Downside Risks: Realistically, factors capable of significantly pulling prices down are limited. Vietnam's production increase is already priced in, with USDA forecasts revised downward from June. Brazil's 2025/26 "on-year" production, while StoneX forecasts 70.7 million bags, assumes normal weather conditions—achievement appears uncertain given current flowering conditions. Even with major speculative liquidation, physical demand should emerge at the 350¢ level.

Key Monitoring Indicators: (1) Southern Brazil Minas/São Paulo rainfall (January-March critical period), (2) ICE certified inventory trend (rapid rally possible if breaks below 300K bags), (3) Brazilian real exchange rate (real weakness could increase short-term supply), (4) Speculative positioning (COT data), (5) Vietnam export pace (whether monthly 2.5-3 million bag level is maintained).
