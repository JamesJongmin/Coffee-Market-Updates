Coffee Futures Market Weekly Update

Weekly Market Trends

November 16, 2025

Headline: Tariff Removal Triggers Drop, Brazil Excluded

The coffee futures market plunged following the Trump administration's sudden tariff removal announcement. December Arabica coffee (KCZ25) closed at 399.80 cents on November 15, down approximately 4% for the week. The price reacted immediately after President Trump signed an executive order on November 14 retroactively removing tariffs on coffee, beef, bananas, tomatoes and other food items effective November 13. However, the 50% tariff on Brazil, the world's largest coffee exporter, remains in place, maintaining structural market tightness.

Executive Summary

December Arabica coffee (KCZ25) closed at 399.80 cents, adjusting about 5.4% from the November 11 high of 422.70 cents. The Trump administration's November 14 tariff removal was based on trade agreements with four South American countries including Ecuador, Guatemala, El Salvador, and Argentina, but excluded Brazil which accounts for 30% of US coffee imports.

President Trump and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent foreshadowed tariff relief to lower coffee prices on November 12-13 and acted on the 14th. However, the 50% tariff on Brazil remains due to political persecution issues surrounding former President Jair Bolsonaro, and US importers continue canceling Brazilian coffee contracts.

The US Supreme Court held oral arguments on November 5 regarding the legality of IEEPA (International Emergency Economic Powers Act) tariffs, with both conservative and liberal justices expressing skepticism, raising the possibility of future tariff invalidation. ICE Arabica stocks have fallen to 406,129 bags, down over 360,000 bags since July, continuing at 19-month lows.

Key News Analysis

1. Trump Tariff Removal - Result of Political Pressure

Nov 14, 2025

Trump Retroactively Removes Coffee, Beef, Fruit Tariffs

President Trump signed an executive order on November 14 retroactively removing tariffs on hundreds of food items including coffee, beef, tomatoes, and bananas effective November 13. The White House stated these items "cannot be produced domestically in sufficient quantities to meet US demand." This action came shortly after Democrats won local elections in Virginia and New Jersey in early November, with voters expressing frustration over food prices. Coconuts, nuts, avocados, pineapples and more were also exempted.

Bloomberg (Nov 14, 2025)

2. Brazil Tariff - Maintained for Political Reasons

Nov 14, 2025

Brazil 50% Tariff Remains in Effect

Despite the Trump administration's tariff removal, the 50% tariff on Brazil remains. When President Trump imposed the 50% tariff on Brazil in July, he cited political persecution of former President Jair Bolsonaro as a key reason. Brazil is the US's second-largest trading partner and the world's top exporter of coffee, soybeans, sugar, and beef. According to Consumer Price Index (CPI) data, coffee prices rose 18.9% year-over-year as of September, while beef rose 14.7%.

CNN (Nov 14, 2025)

3. Supreme Court Tariff Hearing - Invalidation Possible

Nov 5, 2025

Supreme Court Shows Skepticism on IEEPA Tariffs

The US Supreme Court held 2 hours and 40 minutes of oral arguments on November 5 regarding the legality of Trump administration IEEPA tariffs. Both conservative and liberal justices expressed skepticism about using "emergency" authority to take full control of trade policy. Justices Sonia Sotomayor and Chief Justice John Roberts emphasized that tariffs are essentially "taxes" which fall under Congressional authority. If the court rules to invalidate IEEPA tariffs, the government would have to refund approximately $90 billion in collected tariffs, and the Brazil coffee 50% tariff could also be eliminated or refunded.

Comunicaffe (Nov 10, 2025)

4. Brazil Weather - Rainfall Recovery

Nov 11, 2025

Minas Gerais Records 160% of Average Rainfall

Brazil's largest Arabica producing region Minas Gerais received 72.1mm of rainfall in the week ending November 7, reaching 160% of historical average moisture. According to Somar Meteorologia, this is expected to positively impact 2026/27 coffee flowering. StoneX forecast on November 13 that Brazil's 2026/27 coffee production would reach 70.7 million bags (including 47.2 million Arabica), a 29% increase year-over-year.

Barchart (Nov 13, 2025)

5. Inventory Trends - Structural Tightness Continues

Nov 11, 2025

ICE Stock at 406,129 Bags, 19-Month Low

ICE certified Arabica stocks stood at 406,129 bags as of November 11, down over 360,000 bags since late July, maintaining 19-month lows. The decline accelerated as US importers canceled new contracts due to the 50% tariff on Brazilian coffee. Given that about 30% of US coffee imports come from Brazil, stock tightness is expected to continue as long as tariffs remain.

Comunicaffe (Nov 10, 2025)

Deep Dive: Political Economy of Trump's Tariff Policy

This week's Trump administration tariff removal was driven by political pressure rather than economic policy, which deserves close attention. Shortly after Democrats won Virginia and New Jersey local elections in early November with voters expressing frustration over rising grocery prices, the Trump administration reversed course within just days.

According to CNN's analysis, President Trump essentially admitted that tariffs are passed on to consumer prices when he said in a Fox News interview that he would "cut tariffs on coffee." This contradicts his previous claims that "tariffs are paid by foreign countries." Tax Foundation Vice President Erica York noted this was "evidence that Trump and his associates understand tariffs are taxes that raise consumer prices - they've just denied it publicly."

However, the 50% tariff on Brazil remains. This stems not from economic logic but from political support for former President Jair Bolsonaro, creating structural distortions in the US coffee supply chain. Brazil is the dominant #1 supplier accounting for 30% of US coffee imports - more than 10 times larger than Ecuador, Guatemala, and El Salvador combined.

US roasters have already canceled Brazilian coffee contracts and are seeking alternative sources like Peru, Honduras, Mexico, and Ethiopia, but completely replacing Brazil's price competitiveness and supply stability is difficult. This means US consumers will have to pay higher coffee prices long-term.
