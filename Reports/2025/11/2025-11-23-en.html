Key News Analysis

1. Trump Completely Removes Brazil Coffee Tariffs

Nov 21, 2025

40% Tariffs on Brazilian Coffee, Beef, and Fruit Completely Removed

President Trump signed an executive order on Thursday, November 21, completely eliminating the 40% tariffs on Brazilian coffee, beef, cocoa, and fruits. This measure is retroactive to November 13, and tariffs collected during this period may be refunded.

The White House explained this decision was to "ease the grocery price burden on American consumers." Trump had previously removed 10% base tariffs on over 200 agricultural products including coffee on November 14, making Brazilian coffee now completely tariff-free.

The Brazilian Coffee Exporters Council (CECAFE) welcomed this in a statement as a "historic victory after months of intensive efforts to represent Brazilian coffee" and "a victory for the entire coffee agricultural production chain."

Washington Times (Nov 21, 2025)

2. Political Economy Behind Tariff Removal

Nov 14-21, 2025

Trump's Tariff Policy Reverses After Election Losses

This tariff removal was driven more by political pressure than economic logic. Shortly after Democrats won Virginia and New Jersey local elections in early November, with voters expressing frustration over rising grocery prices, the Trump administration reversed course within just days.

According to NPR, US consumers were paying nearly 19% more for roasted coffee and 22% more for instant coffee year-over-year as of September. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick told Fox Business that the President said "it's been 6 months, let's start fresh. If deals aren't happening with small countries, that's fine. Let's focus on economics and bring down prices for these natural resources."

NPR (Nov 14, 2025)

3. Brazil Real Strength and Export Dynamics

Nov 21, 2025

Brazilian Real Strengthens to USD/BRL 5.33, Reducing Export Incentives

The Brazilian Real recorded USD/BRL 5.3332 on November 21, strengthening 1.24% over the past month. While this is slightly weaker than the year's peak of 5.2723 on November 11, it remains 15.44% stronger than the early year level of 6.3051.

According to Comunicaffe, the Brazilian Real hit a 17-month high on November 11, which is suppressing export sales from Brazilian coffee producers. A strong Real reduces export revenue in dollar terms, incentivizing producers to delay sales.

Trading Economics (Nov 21, 2025)

4. ICE Arabica Stock - Historic Low Continues

Nov 11, 2025

ICE Stock at 406,129 Bags, Down 360K from July

ICE monitored Arabica stocks fell to 406,129 bags as of November 11, down over 360,000 bags in just 3.5 months from 760,000 bags at end of July. This sharp decline resulted from US buyers canceling Brazilian coffee contracts due to US tariffs on Brazilian coffee, significantly reducing ICE warehouse receipts.

Brazil normally supplies about 30% of US green coffee imports, making it the dominant #1 supplier. The tariff-induced purchase halt has driven ICE stocks to historic lows, strongly supporting price floors.

Comunicaffe (Nov 11, 2025)

5. Vietnam - Heavy Rains Disrupt Harvest, 16 Dead

Nov 19, 2025

Heavy Rains in Central Vietnam Severely Impact Robusta Harvest

According to Comunicaffe's November 19 report, over 1,500mm of rain fell over three days in central Vietnam, severely damaging major Robusta producing regions. Floods and landslides killed 16 people with 5 missing, while 43,000 homes and 10,000 hectares of farmland were flooded.

Harvest operations in Dak Lak province, the primary Robusta producing region, have been severely delayed. Additional rainfall is forecast for November 20, raising concerns of further crop damage.

Deep Dive: Extreme Backwardation Explained

üìâ Abnormal Price Structure

The coffee futures market currently shows an extremely abnormal price structure. December is at 400 cents while March 2026, just 3 months later, is at 369.45 cents - over 30 cents cheaper. Annualized, this represents an inverse premium of over 30% - extreme backwardation.

1. What is Backwardation?

Normal futures markets show a contango structure, where prices rise for more distant delivery dates. This reflects "carry costs" including storage, financing, and insurance. For example, receiving coffee in March should cost more than December because of 3 months of warehouse fees and financing costs.

Currently, it's the opposite. Backwardation is when nearby delivery contracts are more expensive than distant ones - this is the market screaming "we desperately need coffee right now."

2. Why Is This Happening?

ICE Stock Depletion is the Core Cause

According to Royal Coffee's October analysis, the coffee market has been in continuous backwardation for the past 2 years, with the degree intensifying sharply in recent weeks. The Dec-Mar spread exceeds 20 cents, and Dec-Jul spread is nearly 50 cents.

The direct cause is ICE certified stocks plunging to 406,000 bags. Normal levels should be over 1.5 million bags, but Brazilian tariffs halted coffee flows into US warehouses, depleting stocks.

3. Is the 30-Cent Dec-Mar Gap Rational?

The answer is "rational from a market perspective, but economically irrational."

Why it's rational:

  • Immediate supply shortage: Almost no ICE certified stock available for December delivery. US roasters desperately need product for Christmas season but Brazilian tariffs blocked supply.
  • Time until March: With tariffs removed, there's time for Brazilian volumes to flow in over the next 3 months.
  • 2026/27 crop expectations: Market expects large Brazilian new crop volumes like StoneX's 70.7M bag forecast to start shipping mid-2026.

Why it's irrational:

  • Excessive premium: Annualized backwardation over 30% is historically extremely rare - a sign of "panic buying."
  • Uncertain supply: Brazil producer forward sales are only 62% vs 70% average, and with Real strength, large volumes within 3 months aren't guaranteed.
  • Structural shortage: Volcafe forecasts 5 consecutive years of supply deficit, so sudden March supply abundance has weak foundations.

4. What Happens Next?

Backwardation is self-correcting. High spot premiums attract supply, while low futures prices suppress demand. As tariff removal enables Brazilian volumes to flow in, backwardation should gradually ease.

However, complete normalization requires:

  • ICE stocks recovering to at least 800,000-1,000,000 bags
  • Real weakness accelerating exports
  • 2026/27 crop actually achieving StoneX's bumper forecast
  • Global demand remaining at current levels (no economic slowdown)
